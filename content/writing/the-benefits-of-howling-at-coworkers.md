On a day much like any other day, Sebastian was eating lunch at work. Like his other coworkers, he took his lunch in a small room tucked away in the staff-only restricted area of the airport. Unlike his coworkers, however, Sebastian didn’t eat in the break room with its ancient microwave, half-dead fluorescent light, and rickety chairs; he instead ate a few doors down the hall in the “dog closet”.

Before Sebastian was hired, the closet used to only hold shelves of treats, supplies, and training aids for the more conventional detector dogs, along with other gear that needed to be shoved somewhere. Since the Ministry of Veterans Affairs had set Sebastian up with this detector dog job, the closet had been slowly and partially retrofitted into a space where Sebastian could store some of the things he needed. The most relevant of those right now was the box of mine dog rations that Sebastian had pulled his lunch out of a moment ago, which he was going to supplement with some of the jerky he kept in a back pocket of his vest, but there was also a self-filling water bowl, a blanket he could rest on, and one of the communication books he could use to tell his more conventionally human coworkers things.

Sebastian braced his lunch against a paw and pulled open the pouch. Once he’d gotten a sniff of the inside of the container, he let out an irritated huff. *As if today wasn’t enough bullshit, I grabbed a damn “hamburger” “meal” because* someone *turned off the light in here again.* While the airport had eventually been convinced to add a staff area badge reader at Sebastian’s height (his body was about two and a half feet tall these days, and he’d gotten the access control tag that normally went in employee badges mounted to his collar, so he needed something lower down) and to give his closet a handle he didn’t need to jump for, a light switch he could reach had been “too expensive”. Sebastian huffed again as he remembered that meeting.

As Sebastian ate, his mood didn’t get any better. His tail trimmed out the mental bullet points of all the ways today had sucked so far: it had been raining on the way in, he’d had to stay out of the way of *four* heavy luggage carts since morning, he’d missed a training concealments (which had been bullshit, people don’t pack *that* much perfume), and, a few minutes ago, someone had tried to grab him and pull him off the belt he was inspecting. That last incident was why he was taking an early lunch — turns out, growling and snapping one’s jaws at a passenger led to one’s supervisor “recommending” a break. *Should’ve bit the guy. Called it self-defence. Probably wouldn’t get away with it though …*

As Sebastian got towards the end of his lunch, a bubbling noise off to his left gave him one more thing to be grumpy about: he’d need to get someone to refill his water.

There was a knock at the door, interrupting the dog’s thoughts. Sebastian hadn’t yet found a way to say “come on in\!” in basic dog noises, and no one had picked up on the mine pack signal of *Awooooo Awooooo awoo* (G for “go”) or the full word despite his now-occasional use of them in the hope someone would notice the Morse. So, he finished his current bite of food, trotted over to the door while softly howling out an invite (*maybe they’ll notice*),  and pulled it open to let the person who’d knocked in.

Anna flicked the light on as she stepped in. “You been eating in the dark, Seb?” she asked.

Sebastian nodded.

“Why didn’t you get anyone?”

Sebastian dipped his head and neck in the best approximation of a shrug he could pull off with his dog-like body. He’d accepted sometimes eating in the dark because people didn’t read signs, just like he’d accepted many other inconveniences of his shape, his working conditions, and his living situation.

Sebastian had always been good at accepting things.

“Thought I’d let you know that I gave the lady who grabbed you a real thorough search and Piotr signed off on the max fine for some fruit.”

Sebastian nodded and let his tail wag once to hint at a small smile.

“Anyway, he wants you on cargo this afternoon but he’ll get it if you want to go home early,” Anna continued. Then, she paused.

“So, this is really awkward, since you’ve been here for, what, over half a year now? and I never thought to ask, but … where *do* you live, anyway?”

Sebastian walked over to his phrasebook and turned its stiff laminated pages to the letters, where he pointed his way through “A-N-I-M-L S-H-L-T-R” with his nose and front paws. It was awkward slow going, even with the over half a decade of using such systems he had under his belt.

He turned back to look at Anna, awaiting her response. She was doing something with her phone. “The city one?” she asked. “All the way across town?”

Sebastian nodded.

“How’re you getting there?”

Sebastian had vehicles generally as a pre-defined word, but he needed to be more specific here, so he tapped out “B-U-S.”

Anna soon turned her phone to Sebastian to show him his usual route to and from work. “Like that?”

Sebastian sat to make looking at the screen easier, then nodded.

“And I thought I had a long commute\! That’s rough if you miss the connection, too — is that why you’re always here early?”

Sebastian turned to reply. “SOME VEHICLE NO TAKE \[I/ME\]” He finished his sentence by turning back to sit in front of his coworker.

Anna looked down at Sebastian, studying his short black fur, droopy ears, and black eyes, seemingly looking for something. Sebastian looked back up at her, trying to make out the expression on her face. Since starting at the airport, Sebastian had gotten the impression that Anna, with her carefully applied makeup, short black hair, near-perpetual friendly smile, and soft, calm voice, did a very good job at being the friendly customs officer who just needs to ask you a few questions before you get on your way. Now, though, Sebastian was pretty sure he was seeing confusion and … guilt?

*Taptaptap.* “Looks like the storm’ll be back tonight, so, if you’re staying, do you, uh, want a ride home? I can’t do it every day, but …”

Sebastian barked quietly once and nodded.

“I’ll meet you here after work, then… And …” Anna searched for what she was trying to say next.

“God, sorry,” she fumbled, “I realized — with that lady, Piotr had threatened battery charges but decided not to and I asked him later and he’d said he wasn’t sure the intent would stick because,” Anna waved a hand at Sebastian’s shape, “and it made me realize that even though I know you’re a person and can understand me, a lot of the time I just see a big dog and so does everyone else and, like, I’m sure it’s made things really rough for you and … I’m sorry.”

This wasn’t the first time Sebastian had gotten this sort of apology, and it wouldn’t be the last. Sometimes these realizations even led to little improvements, but being seen as a dog that was sometimes human was something Sebastian had mostly accepted. Sebastian was good at accepting things.

The man in the body of a dog, a hero of the post-war forest de-mining efforts, turned to the first page of words he had.  “\[NORMAL/TYPICAL/STANDARD\]”

“Doesn’t mean it’s OK.”

“NORMAL“ A twist of the head back for the punctuation mark that could be a comma or a period when he talked like this and didn’t want to put effort into precise symbols. “\[ACCEPTABLE/FINE\].”

“It’s not, though. Like, you’ve been taking breaks here alone in a closet and no one even *thought* to invite you to the Christmas party last week and —”

Sebastian made a small ‘wrf’ noise to cut Anna off. “NORMAL,”  *flip fiip* “\[PERIOD\]” *flip* “ACCEPTABLE” *flip flip* “\[PERIOD\]” he insisted. He wasn’t feeling up for one of these conversations right now — today had been long enough and it was (probably) just past noon. Not to mention that the cargo belts tended to get dusty and make him sneeze, to add to today’s pile of annoyances.

Anna sighed. “You’re really OK with people just not seeing you as a person a lot of the time? That would be so lonely, I don’t know if I could … I’m so sorry, Sebastian.”

Sebastian sighed, and realized he maybe shouldn’t be a complete dick here but try to elaborate. He didn’t mind Anna, for one thing. “ONLY CHOICE. NO METHOD CHANGE.” A second punctuation twist for a more substantial break.  “BODY. ONLY LEG. SPEAK DIFFICULT.”

Anna sat down on the floor, not appearing to mind the crumbs and shed fur despite her usual care about her clothes. “I know you don’t like it when people pet you, but … can I give you a hug?”

Sebastian considered this, and decided he didn’t mind. It wouldn’t make the not being people problem worse the way petting did. He nodded, able to look her in the eye now that they were both sitting.

Anna wrapped her arms behind Sebastian’s front legs and as far up his body as she could reach and gently squeezed him, pressing her face into his fur. Sebastian let his tail wag — this was nice, he had to admit. *Shouldn’t get used to this,* he thought. *She’ll forget soon.*

Anna stood back up and dusted off her pants. She looked down at Sebastian. “I … don’t know what else to say, just … yeah. Let me know if you need anything? And I’ll see you tonight?”

Sebastian remembered what he’d noticed before Anna had come in. He stood and pushed his water bowl towards his coworker with his nose.

Anna looked at the bowl, thought for a bit, and realized what he was getting at. “Right, it’s probably time to refill that. Come to think of it, I’ll try to get that added to the maintenance schedule.”

*Well, there’s my little improvement from this one,* Sebastian thought. He turned to finish the rest of his food once Anna had left. *A little bit of hope can be okay, I guess.*

By the time Sebastian heard the click of Anna’s low heels returning with the tank for his water bowl, he’d nearly finished eating, and was just munching on the jerky he’d pulled from his snack pouch.

Anna put the water tank back in and said “See you around, Seb,” before leaving, turning the light off as she did so.

A moment later, she pulled the door back open to turn the light on again with a “Fuck. Sorry.”

Sebastian just kept eating. This sort of thing happened sometimes. It was fine.

What else could it be?

Sebastian spent the afternoon trying to ensure people weren’t sending anything undeclared and explosive- or drug-smelling in cargo. He’d had a boring afternoon — all he’d found was a box of weed that really should’ve been caught by one of the humans with how obvious the stench was.

Once it was time to clock out, he didn’t just walk out of the airport like always, but went to his closet to wait for Anna. When he got there, she was already there, though. He hoped she hadn’t been waiting long, but decided that that wasn’t worth trying to communicate with more than an apologetic look.

“You need to get anything before we leave?” Anna asked him. Sebastian shook his head and waited to see where Anna would go.

“Well, uh, follow me, I guess,” Anna stumbled.

Sebastian followed Anna to the airport’s employee parking, getting very wet in the process. He walked around Anna’s small gray car, trying to decide which space he’d have the easiest time squeezing into, and settled on the back row instead of the trunk — ideally on the back seat. He shook the water from the storm off as best he could before hopping up through the door Anna opened once she’d noticed he’d wanted in.

“You good back there?” Anna asked, once she’d started the car. Sebastian barked once, Anna drove off, and the ride to the shelter passed with the only noise being the radio, as Sebastian didn’t have anything he could say. Anna didn’t seem to want

When Anna had reached the shelter, she parked and let Sebastian out. He hopped out of her car and walked to the front door of the shelter, where the dog door read his collar tags and opened for him.

Sebastian’s mood perked up when he noticed Arthur, one of the shelter workers he’d gotten to know since moving in, was at the front desk. The dog howled a quick “Hi” to the man, who whistled one back.

The front door opened before Sebastian left the lobby. “Friend of yours?” Arthur asked.

Sebastian looked back and saw Anna had followed him in. He wasn’t sure why she’d done that, and he also wasn’t sure if “friend” was a good term. So, he told Arthur “coworker”, moving from quiet howls to whines as he remembered he should keep his voice down.

“Coworker, you said?” Arthur asked. “Not sure I quite got that.”

Sebastian nodded. Arthur had only been working at the shelter for a few months, but he’d been getting to know the de-mining dogs and was making an effort to learn to talk to them and get to know their culture, as it were. It was one of the reasons Sebastian liked him.

Anna came up to the desk now that she could tell that she wasn’t interrupting something. “Hi, I’m Anna, I work with Sebastian at the airport, yeah,” she said.

“Arthur,” the grey-haired man said, standing from his chair and brushing what was left of his hair back, much to the annoyance of the non-person dog he’d been petting. “Glad you’re here — the army wing doesn’t get many visitors.”

“So, this is a really stupid question, but how’d you get ‘coworker’ from all those whines and howls?”

“It’s the Morse code thing?” Arthur replied, equally confused.

“The what thing?”

“You know, the ancient code from some really early texting systems? They taught it to all the de-miners so they could still sort of talk.”

“This is the first time I’m hearing about this,” Anna said. She looked down at Sebastian. “Why didn’t you say anything? This sounds like it’d make things a lot easier for everyone.”

Sebastian replied with a sequence of noises.

“Yeah, I’ll pass a message for you, but I need you to slow down.”

There was a longer sequence of notes of systematically varying lengths.

“He says no one responded so he thought you didn’t care to learn,” Arthur said.

More whining, interrupted by a “Slow down. Again.” from Arthur.

“And that you didn’t even catch the codes — he means the really short ones the Army set up.”

When Sebastian looked at Anna, she looked horrified. “No one told me anything about this, I swear\! I thought all we had was that book\!”

Sebastian wasn’t sure how to respond to this, so he waited.

“So, uh, where can I get these codes?” Anna continued. “That’s probably more helpful than the second big apology of the day.”

Sebastian nodded his agreement, still processing the possibility that some (all?) of the officers at the airport might not have known he was trying to talk, and that those few signals he’d been asked to use were them trying to roll their own system because they didn’t know about the old one. It made an unfortunate amount of sense. *Fuck.*

“You want me to send them to you or get you paper copies?” Arthur asked.

“Both, probably. Juggling documents is a pain,” Anna said.

“Sure is,” Arthur agreed. “Mind waiting around for a few minutes? Have to have someone at the desk and Irene had to go help with food. I can meet you by … the big symbol books in the yapping room — 1A — so you two can keep talking.”

“I can wait, sure,” Anna agreed, “if someone can show me there.”

Sebastian stepped out of the lobby and looked back, then used the “follow me” yip he’d managed to teach at work to make sure Anna was coming.

*At least they picked that up even though they didn’t know it was part of the system,* he thought as he led Anna down sidewalks past kennels full of more typical dogs and then through a door and series of hallways very familiar to him and entirely unfamiliar to her. *Probably going to want to overhaul the short codes for airport work, too. “Unattended” could be good to have, maybe.*

Even Anna could hear the room Sebastian was taking her to before she could see it — now that she knew what she was listening for, she could make out de-mining dogs having conversations.

Shortly after the din became noticeable, Sebastian led Anna around a corner and into one of the rooms that had been made to allow the former soldiers living at the city animal shelter to socialize or other things that weren’t just laying around. This one contained a bunch of stuff meant for communicating with humans — fairly thick binders of symbols, including one that looked to be names and photos of the mine dogs — along with other tools like tiles one could rearrange to spell something more permanently and a number of common words painted repeatedly low on the wall. It also contained plenty of soft spots to lay down or sit on, and several bowls of water and snacks.

Off to the side, separated from all the dogs by a wall of glass and a door (with, like most doors on this wing, a large dog door) was a room marked “1B: Veterans’ Wing Reception”. It contained a man keeping an eye on some screens and glancing at something on the other side of the wall occasionally.

The room had around ten formerly-human mine-clearing dogs lounging around and talking. They were all roughly the same size as Sebastian, and most of them shared his short black fur, though one dog had short brown fur and another had longer gray fur. Several of them turned to look at Sebastian and Anna as they walked in.

Sebastian didn’t wait before starting his introduction, going much faster than he had in the lobby. “Anna from work,” he said. “Gave me a ride.” He paused, a bit embarrassed to admit the next part but knowing it had to be said. “Learned today she didn’t know about code. Maybe all don’t know.”

His announcement earned Sebastian a few playful swats from his fellow veterans’ paws. “So, not dicks?” one of his fellows asked, and another went for some gloating: “Told you to remind them.” One of the dogs left the room when he heard the news to get one of Sebastian’s former squadmates, while Sebastian laid down on a pillow, leaving Anna to wait alone.

Anna navigated around the maze of pillows on the floor to get to the clear area near the room’s word-books and stood there, taking in the hubbub. She was unsure what to do, especially since the dogs didn’t seem to be paying much attention to her. Soon, though, a dog came into the room from a side entrance and went to Anna, who he led over to one of the word-books through some gentle tugs on her pant legs.

“I JANEK,” the dog introduced himself. “SAME SQUAD SEBASTIAN.” Now Anna understood what that book of dog photos was for.

Janek then led Anna over to one of the collections of uncommon words to tell her “CONDOLENCES”.

“Condolences on what?*”* Anna asked as she moved to a different binder. When Sebastian heard this, he looked up, noticed Janek, and walked over to see what he was up to.

Sebastian arrived in time for Janek’s answer. “YOUR MINE-DOG IS DUMBASS.”

Janek then turned to Sebastian to give him a lick with his tail wagging, which Sebastian returned. The dogs walked off together, chatting, though Anna couldn’t understand them. (“So, what happened?”, “She drove me home and heard me talk to Arthur and it clicked — and I got another ‘whoops, dog’ earlier”, “What about?”, “Dark closet”, “You’ve gotta stand up for yourself more, Sniffer.”, Sebastian sighed. “I know …” …).

Anna stood around as the conversation she couldn’t yet understand moved to the other side of the room. She thought she understood a bit of what Sebastian’s life was like, now that she was surrounded by conversations she couldn’t join in on, though her case of that was temporary. In the lobby, Sebastian had been using a series of noises of different lengths, but here, she heard many warbling notes that … maybe were using pitch changes instead of pausing? Was that what Arthur meant by slowing down? She’d have to ask later.

As she stood and waited for Arthur, Anna noticed a brown dog who was napping (or maybe sulking?) on a bed that has been put within easy view of the woman at “reception”. He was wearing a muzzle, a large plastic cone buckled around his neck that nearly enveloped his face, and soft shoes vet-wrapped to his paws. “What’s up with him?” Anna asked, pointing. “Did he get hurt?”

A dog she hadn’t met before came by to answer the question.  “ROBERT H. TRY GO” the dog launched into a dance of snout taps that literally meant “UP, UP, UP, UP, UP, UP, UP, UP” The message concluded with a normally delivered  “AGAIN”.

“So, wait, as in …?”

It was then that Anna realized Arthur had arrived with the code papers she’d wanted. “As in tried to book an express train to heaven, swallow a bullet, that sort of thing, y’know?” Arthur explained. “We’re waiting for the doctor and therapist to get here tomorrow.”

Anna slowly walked over to near Robert’s bed. Communication codes could wait. “Hey, Robert. I don’t know what’s going on and I don’t know you but … do you want a hug?”

Robert’s low, rumbling tired growl was all the answer Anna needed. She went back to Arthur. “I hope he’ll, uh, be OK soon.”

“I do too,” Arthur said. “It’s always rough when one of  the guys gets that far down in a hole — but at least there’s a good group here; they look out for each other.”

Anna didn’t know what to say to that, so Arthur moved on once the silence had become awkward. “Right, so, here’s the basic code, the shorthand, and some notes we’ve put together,” he said, passing the paper. “I’ll just zap those over to you too…”

Arthur passed Anna the files, and Anna flipped through the paper copies. “I’ll pass these along at work,” she said. “Thanks for getting all this together.”

“Not a problem. They’re good people, these mine dogs. Gotta help them out when I can.”

Anna folded the papers and tucked them into her pocket. “Sebastian\!” she called on a sudden impulse, “I’ll be back here around 8 so we can meet with Piotr before our shift.”

A dog — Janek, Anna saw — came by, said something short, then left the room. “He went to pass the message,” Arthur explained.

It didn’t take long for Janek to return and ask Arthur for a relay. “Sniffer says he’ll be out front for that,” Janek said.

“Sniffer?” Anna asked.

“Sebastian,” Arthur clarified, saving Janek from needing to provide that context.

“Do you call him that because he has a good nose?”

Janek barked in a way that might have been a laugh. “Once he tracked to a heap of pepper and shoved his nose in. Lots of sneezing.”

Once Arthur had finished translating that, Janek added “Don’t tell him I told you.”

“Sure,” Anna said. “I’ll see you tomorrow. Could you take me to the exit?”

She’d directed that remark at Arthur, but it was Janek who guided her back out to the parking lot.

The following morning, Sebastian sat at attention in his boss’s office (eyes forward, head level, front legs straight, tail straight back). It seemed like the right thing to do for the conversation that was starting up.

“So, let me get this straight,” Piotr said from behind his desk as he tapped on his keyboard. “There’s this whole other system you can use to talk to us, which looks much better than pointing at words, and we didn’t know about it until last night … oh, there it is, I somehow missed those attachments.”

“Yep,” Anna said.

“And Sebastian,” his boss emphasized the name, “you didn’t think to try to tell us this before now because?”

“He said last night —” Anna began.

“No, I want to hear this from Sebastian.”

Sebastian stood to go to the word book Anna had grabbed for this meeting. “No,” Piotr said, standing up.

 Sebastian paused, and Piotr went to pick up the book and take it behind his desk. “I want to hear this from you, Sebastian,” he emphasized, holding up the Morse code chart he’d had put on his desk. “It’ll be awkward — I think we’ll go a letter at a time and I’ll be repeating everything back to you as I write it down, but I want to hear this from you.”

Sebastian turned around, sat, and was about to start talking when Piotr realized something. “Do you want Anna here for this?”

Sebastian made an uncertain noise. This was going to be awkward either way.

“That’s a ‘maybe’?”

Sebastian barked a yes, the way he’s been answering questions here, but followed it up with a “Y\[es\].”

“Well, Anna, you can stay or leave if you want. This might take a while.”

Anna took a seat and settled in.

“When you didn’t seem to notice I was trying to talk,” Sebastian explained, “and didn’t pick up on even the short codes, I thought you didn’t care to learn. Didn’t want to work to talk to me. Many people don’t.”

Piotr looked at the message and frowned. “And it didn’t cross your mind to remind us you could do this at any point in these last, how long’s it been, seven months?”

Sebastian’s sitting at attention collapsed into an ashamed look. “Other minedogs said I should. I didn’t want to complain. Work was OK wo q.”

“Your work was OK without talking, query — as in, that’s a question — you said?”

“Y\[es\].”

“It was, but I’m sure it would have been better if we’d talked more,” Piotr said.

As he considered how to reply to Sebastian, Piotr noticed the time. “Anna, go let everyone know we’re having a meeting before lunch and that I’ll be in here a while, but I’m interruptible if it’s urgent. Nothing special for stations.”

Anna walked out of the supervisor’s office, making sure to close the door behind her.

“Now, Sebastian,” the mine-dog’s boss continued, “to get back to my point, you just put up with this,” he said, holding up the word-book, “because you didn’t want to complain. Because … why? What did you think would happen?”

Sebastian had spent much of last night thinking about that question and overcoming his worry that answering it might make his fears come true. He focused on his posture, trying to mask his emotions, and was glad that he already had to talk so slowly that it’d be hard to detect the hesitancy in his voice. “Didn’t want to be a problem. Thought it wouldn’t work.

“And,” Sebastian took a deep breath, giving up on keeping up appearances, “scared you might not want me anymore.”

“You thought we might fire you for … being an inconvenience … if you asked us to … learn to understand you,” Piotr let that hang in the air.

*Fuck, it sounds* really *dumb when he puts it like that,* Sebastian thought. “Y\[es\]. Was wrong.”

“Is there anything someone did or said that made you think that I should know about?” Sebastian’s boss asked. In a less concerned voice, he added “Also, we can speed up a little bit, I’m starting to get the hang of this.”

Sebastian  “Everyone sees dog, not person, sometimes. Here like everywhere. No one to blame. Is what is. Used to it.”

Piotr drummed his fingers on his desk. “And you’re probably used to a lot of things, I’m guessing. You’ve been making it work here, or just deciding everything’s fine even if it isn’t, am I right?”

“I’m good at that.” Sebastian agreed. Something more than a yes had seemed in order.

Piotr sighed. He stood up, rolled up one of the sleeves of his white button-down shirt so it wouldn’t get shed on, and sat down on the floor next to Sebastian, draping an arm around him.

“Sebastian, I want you to complain more, and not just stuff that’s a problem for us like when we fixed it so you could badge in. For one thing, you’re an important part of the team and I don’t want you to just put up with the nonsense we’re doing when we sometimes forget you’re a person. I care about making this a good place to work for everyone here — that includes you. You being in the body of a dog is a pretty big adjustment for all of us, not gonna lie, but we can’t stop causing problems for you if you never say anything. Please. Help me make things better for you. And let me assure you I will **not** be firing you for speaking up.”

Piotr looked at Sebastian’s face, trying to read the emotions there.  “And … if that’s hard to accept, I’ve been thinking we might get more de-mining dogs in here once we get more budget, so I don’t want the team developing bad habits your squad-mates won’t put up with because you never said anything. So, maybe if complaining for yourself is hard, try complaining for them?”

Sebastian leaned against his boss. “I’ll try.” *Why does asking for things have to be scarier than nearly getting my leg blown off?*

“You said you’ll try, right?” Piotr said.

Sebastian nodded.

“Good. I hope I got through to you some, because the only other idea I have is to send you to those really awkward effective communication HR trainings until this sinks in, and that might actually make you tear me to shreds when you get back,” Sebastian’s boss said jokingly. He stood, though he didn’t sit back down behind his desk, instead beginning to pace around his office.

“So, I’ve been thinking of a plan for this,” Piotr waved around the charts he picked up as he passed his desk, “but before I start going through that, anything you want me to know? Or any other complaints?”

Sebastian did have a thought. “Should change short codes for here.” Then, he took a deep breath (*I did just say I’d try to complain more…*) and said, “and breaks in break room?”

“Good point — do you already know what you want to change?”

“Y\[es\]. Show in book.”

Piotr put the word-book down on the floor. “And … I’ll think about ways to squeeze you into the break room. Maybe free up some floor space.”

“Longer couch,” Sebastian suggested.

“Guess it’s my turn to have missed something obvious for months,” Piotr said with a chuckle. “So, I’m guessing that you’ve got something better for T than ‘trip’. … ‘Explosive’ on E can probably stay, though, that’s an important one, yeah?”

The two men worked out the changes to Sebastian’s code while Piotr explained what he had planned for today’s meeting and checked that Sebastian would be OK with it.

When they’d finished, Piotr squatted down to look at Sebastian. “I’m betting that this has been a rough morning, so I’m leaving you unassigned until that meeting. Go do some searches if you want, or go relax somewhere. Your call.”

Piotr opened the door to let Sebastian out. The dog disappeared down the hallway. *I’ll go check some mail*, he decided. *That doesn’t involve people, at least.*

Sebastian sat, only mostly at attention this time, next to Piotr, waiting for the sets of Morse code charts and hastily edited lists of abbreviations to make their way around the circle of customs officers gathered near the big X-ray machine, where they always held team meetings. He turned his head, trying to take in everyone despite his missing eye. Most people were looking at the charts with some confusion.

“So, I’d like to start this meeting off by asking Sebastian to introduce himself.”

Sebastian howled out a “Hi, I’m Sebastian,” keeping his speed down and making sure to enunciate clearly. He could see some of his coworkers realizing what was going on, and noticed some of them working their way down the chart or through the tree to find letters as he got into his name.

“One thing,” Anna said from where she stood a few people away from Sebastian. “When I was at the shelter last night, I heard the other mine-dogs talking a lot faster and more, I guess, musically than that, and I’m thinking we should try to get used to that right away so we’re not making Seb talk realll slowwww liiiiiike thiiiiiis all the time.”

Piotr blinked. “Oh, uh, good point, let’s try that again at speed?”

Sebastian said “Hi, I’m Sebastian,” again, but now at a more comfortable rate. He let some of the mine-dog accent’s pitch contouring and slurring come through, though he didn’t subject his coworkers to the full force of those adaptations right away.

“To spell out everything, Sebastian has this code I’ve given you all copies of — and I’ll send it around later — so he could actually talk to people back in the Army, and I only found out about it this morning thanks to, uh, a series of miscommunications.

“Since Sebastian’s an important part of the team here, I don’t want him left out or forced to point at words or hint at things to get his points across. So, I’m asking everyone to put some effort into learning this system so we can work together more effectively. This’ll be a mutual effort, so feel free to ask Sebastian to tell you what something’s called or slow down or whatnot. That being said, the book of words we’ve been using is reserved for teaching this Morse code or emergencies for the next week so there isn’t too much cheating.

“Now, I’ll open the floor for questions or suggestions — and that includes changes to the abbreviations list, since he and I only adjusted that for airport use this morning. My door’s open if you think of something later, as always.”

Many of the people in the circle still seemed to be taking in the revelations about Sebastian and what he could do, so the end of Piotr’s speech was met by the shuffling of papers. Then, someone piped up: “Maybe we could get a recording of everyone saying their name and Sebastian repeating it back so we can know if he’s trying to talk to us.”

“R\[odger\]. M\[oving\],” Sebastian replied, stepping into the circle. He paused to let people look up what he’d just said.

“Oh, *that’s* what those noises I keep hearing you make mean\!” one of the older agents exclaimed. “Can’t believe I didn’t pick on ‘rodger’ from context, at least.”

Sebastian then went around, finally saying all his coworkers’ names to them after many months of work at the airport.

“Are there some recordings we can practice on?” Sylvia asked. Sebastian turned to her — he hadn’t expected her to say anything, since he knew her as someone who forgot his humanity more than most (which he couldn’t be too bothered by — she had enough wrinkles and white hair to make people think she should have already retired twice by now, after all). “Maybe someone’s made an audiobook by dogs.”

“Can make some or ask other mine-dogs,” Sebastian said. He then repeated the message more clearly since he could see he’d surprised Sylvia by responding.

“I only got half of that, but I think you said you could … something or ask other dogs? I’m guessing to make some.”

Sebastian nodded.

From across the circle, Pavel sighed. “Honesrly, this code thing seems like a huge pain for everyone just for the sake of one dog. Doesn’t the current system work fine?”

Piotr cut in. “No, it didn’t work fine — it was the only option we thought we had. And Sebastian deserves better than that. You’ve seen how good having that nose with a brain attached is.”

Sebastian managed to pick up Pavel’s muttered “knew that dog would be a pain.” Sometimes, enhanced hearing had its drawbacks.

There was a tension in the room now. Sebastian took a deep breath and decided now was a time to say something he hadn’t had a chance to say to everyone before. Some part of him worried that this was a mistake, that he should just accept the problem instead, that it would be somehow dangerous to speak up. He did his best to ignore those worries as he “wrf”ed to get everyone’s attention.

“All,” he said, stopping to look over at the worst offenders, “stop calling me a good boy like I’m a dog. I’m not a dog.” Sebastian spoke slowly and carefully, pausing between words to let people catch up or whisper to each other to sort out what he’d said.

“Now I can say that to us together.”

“Thank you for reminding us of that, Sebastian,” Piotr said. “I’m glad you said something to remind us you’re a human being under that fur, now that we can understand each other better.”

Sebastian looked up and around and found a mix of contemplative faces and people checking their phones or watches. So, he proceeded to the final point of Piotr’s plan: “Q\[uery\] lunch.”

“It has gotten to lunchtime,” Piotr agreed.

Sebastian walked out of the room. He was in the mood for one of the good field rations — the ones he saved for particularly bad or good days.

As he opened the dog closet, he found it was dark. *Dammit, not again.*

Sebastian turned around and decided to bother someone about that. Maybe that fear of trying to change things instead of accepting them would go away if this kept working. He turned to go back to the human break room, intending to bug the first coworker he saw.

He almost caved on that plan when that first coworker turned out to be Pavel, who walked out of that room and began walking towards Sebastian before he could start moving. “Pavel, closet lights pl\[ea\]s\[e\],” he said, nearly at speed like he’d been asked to.

Pavel stopped when he reached Sebastian. “You want something from me?”

Sebastian nodded.

Pavel sighed. “Is it quick?”

Sebastian nodded. “Y\[es\].”

“What is it?”

Sebastian looked at the darkness and repeated “closet lights.”

Pavel followed Sebastian’s gaze. “Someone turned the lights off again, I’m guessing?”

Nod. Bark. “Y\[es\].”

Pavel flipped the lights on. “They should’ve just given you another switch,” he grumbled, as he walked away. “Don’t know why they didn’t.”

“Too expensive,” Sebastian replied, not expecting Pavel to understand.

Pavel kept going, almost certainly to the bathrooms, and Sebastian went back into his closet to get some food.

As he ate, he thought that accepting the fact that he shouldn’t just accept things so much might be the right thing to do. He’d accepted much worse, after all, and he was good at accepting things.

---

The next month proved to be an awkward experience for Sebastian. He found himself in charge of teaching people to talk to him, and being in charge didn’t fit much better on him now than it had in the few times he’d been made to try it during Army training.

Sebastian had gotten clear of the largest project quickly, at least. When he’d passed on the idea of making practice tracks for listening to de-mining dogs, he’d encountered broad interest. Recordings had been made quickly, and people from Sebastian’s coworkers to shelter staff and even to people across the country who were working with mine-dogs who’d had somewhere else they could go. Sebastian was also glad that Robert H. (who’d been a squad leader) had latched onto the effort and found that it kept him out of a muzzle and cone — he said it helped him feel less forgotten. (It probably helped that he slid some of his poetry into the exercises when he could.)

At work, though, Sebastian was the only de-mining dog, and so he had to be the expert. The calm intensity of his shifts, of finding and tracking unusual scents and of watching for the little subtleties of movement that told him something wasn’t right, of being the one who was good at noticing things, was shattered by the humans around him wanting to hear from him. The background level of “Hey, Seb, what’s that?” and “Can you read this for me?” and “So, how was your weekend?” made it harder to work, compared to when he’d been mostly ignored.

He accepted the hassle, though. For one thing, he’d asked for this, and it *was* nice to be able to (sort of, they were still learning, but it had only been a month) talk to his coworkers.

Sebastian had even come to accept the feeling of everyone staring at him expectantly that came with reading the pre-shift briefing these days. Anna had suggested he should be the one to read them (with someone else reading them again afterwards so people could make sure they’d understood) so that everyone could always have some exposure to Sebastian’s speech. But he accepted those eyes looking down at him, just as he’s accepted many other parts of the job before this.

Sebastian had always been good at accepting things.

Now, though, there were fewer people around, and they weren’t paying attention to him as he laid down and considered these new developments. Today, for lack of other evening plans, he’d volunteered to stay late to deal with a flood of delayed flights along with a few other day shift folks.

“Seb?” Anna asked.

Seb barked uncertainly.

“You in for beers?”

Seb stood up. “Y\[es\]\!” he answered, feeling his tail wag. “Been a while. Home n\[o/egative\] beers.”

Amir looked Sebastian over. “Will you fit in my car?” he asked. “You’re pretty, uh, long.”

“I can go in the trunk,” Sebastian volunteered.

Thus, a pack of customs officials, one of whom was shaped like a dog, found themselves at a nearby restaurant and bar, intending to drink their overtime pay. That plan quickly hit a snag right after the group had walked in through the thick wooden doors of the mildly upscale chain restaurant they’d gone drinking at before.

“We don’t allow dogs in here,” said the host.

“Get ID f\[ront\]l\[eft\], pl\[ea\]s\[e\],” Sebastian asked the group. Amir pulled open the front-left pocket on the ex-soldier’s vest and found Sebastian’s ID, which he gave to Sebastian. Sebastian walked forward and offered his ID to the host.

The host studied the ID and noted that the picture did, in fact, match the dog standing beside him. “Don’t know what this is about, or how well trained you think he is, we still don’t allow dogs in here.”

The host offered the ID back to Amir, who put it back in Sebastian’s vest. “He’s human,” Anna tried to explain. “He got turned into this by the Army so he could clear smart mines.”

“No dogs,” the host insisted.

Sebastian had always been good at accepting things. He turned to leave, the two long notes of “m\[oving\]” carrying the weight of resignation.

“Ride —” he began asking as he reached the door.

“No, no, you turn your fuzzy ass back around,” Pavel interrupted firmly. Sebastian did so.

“Team beers means team beers, you had to deal with those damn London flights same as the rest of us, you’re staying,” Pavel explained. He strode up to the host, fully sank into the “I’m not asking” voice that was his preferred way of dealing with people who didn’t want their bags checked, and said “I need to speak to someone in charge here.”

“I’ll get the manager in just a moment, sir, if you’re willing to wait,” the host said, “but I don’t think she’ll tell you any different.”

Despite Pavel’s attempts to explain the situation and Sebastian chiming in, talking to the manager didn’t do anything but make the refusal of service more polite. “Look, I get it,” she said, “your friend’s *technically* human, but I don’t want the health department asking questions if they see him or notice a bunch of fur. Sorry.”

“Well, we’re leaving them,” Pavel said. No one questioned him.

As the team found and drove to another place that would hopefully serve all of them and was, at Sebastian’s request, “not too loud”. Pavel twisted around to face the trunk  “Why don’t I get you your ID — not sure why that’s where it’s a pain to reach but OK — when we park and you’ll walk up first with it in your mouth. Maybe that’ll help.”

“Don’t need ID much,” Sebastian explained. “Don’t want to clutter solo pockets.”

Pavel grunted understanding.

Soon, the group arrived at a new restaurant. This place looked like better days had seen it, and like it hadn’t been renovated since before the *previous* war. Sylvia swore the food was good here, even if the service wasn’t the best.

Anna opened the door for Sebastian, who walked in while everyone else waited outside for a bit. He barked to get someone’s attention

“No dogs,” the nearest server to the door  said on autopilot before he took in the rest of Sebastian’s appearance. “Wait, hold on, give me that,” he said, taking a hold of Sebastian’s ID.

Sebastian let go of the ID, which the server studied carefully. “Sebastian, huh?”

“Hi,” Sebastian said, knowing the server wouldn’t understand him.

“Didn’t know they gave veteran IDs to dogs now,” the server commented.

The rest of the group had come in to hear that. “Not a dog,” Pavel corrected. “Seb’s human.”

“He’s got some extra legs for a human,” the server commented. Then, something clicked in their brain. “This the thing where they made a bunch of people look like wolves to deal with those death circles?”

Sebastian nodded. “Yes,” he said.

“They didn’t change you back?”

Sebastian nodded.

“Damm, that sucks,” the server said.

“We’re here to get some drinks,”  Pavel said.

The server looked at Sebastian’s ID again. “So, were you planning on sitting in a chair somehow?”

“Floor is fine,” Sebastian said, with Anna translating.

The server stepped away from the host stand. “I’ll go check with the boss, but I think we can get you in.”

The server came back after a minute and leaned down to give Sebastian his ID, which he took into his mouth. “We’ve got a big table in the back for y’all, and we can put some plates on the floor, but my boss said, quote ‘if he bites anyone or pisses on the floor, he’s out.’”

“Duh,” Sebastian said, and Sylvia passed that along. Then, she added, “If you’re worried about people getting bit, it’s me you need to watch out for, not him.” Sebastian had heard a few stories from her youth, so he could guess that that wasn’t a joke.

Everyone settled in and got their drinks. Sebastian’s beer and water came in soup bowls, and the chicken and fries he’d ordered (after running through the list of spices he wasn’t supposed to eat) went onto the ground, while everyone else’s orders were on the table.

Once Sebastian had lapped at his drink enough for the alcohol to kick in, he said, “Thanks for standing up for me, Pavel. Thought you didn’t like me.”

Pavel took a long sip of his beer and set it down on the table with a thud. “I thought you working detection would just be a pain in the ass for the rest of us and wouldn’t help much — and I’m still deciding if I was wrong about that. That doesn’t mean I don’t like you, and it definitely doesn’t mean I think you should have been kicked out like that.”

“Is what is. Used to it.”

“Fuck, did they lose the part of your brain that doesn’t put up with people’s shit when they made you into a big fuzzball?”

Sebastian lapped up a bit more beer. “Never really had one. Good at getting used to stuff.”

“You sure are.”

The conversation kept flowing, almost as it would have if Sebastian had brought other mine-dogs here: stories, music recommendations, gripes about the world, and so on. Sebastian even joined in on the complaining once he’d reached the end of his beer.

Pavel noticed Sebastian’s empty bowl. “Want another?”

“Y\[es\].”

Pavel flagged down a waiter. “One more beer for me and one more for him.”

When the second drink arrived, Sebastian took it slow. It has been … he wasn’t entirely sure how long … since he’d gotten drunk, and he remembered that he got drunk faster as a dog.

Once the officers were well into their second hour of drinking, they took up tossing peanuts they’d ordered down to Sebastian and watching him try to catch them in his mouth. Sebastian, for his part, was drunk enough to find this funny and not insulting.

The alcohol had, by then, loosened Anna’s tongue enough that she asked a question she’d had on her mind since she’d first met her furriest teammate. “Hey, Seb, I was wondering, if you’re cool with going into it … what happened to your eye?”

“Usually I leave it at ‘we fucked up’ but … fuck it.”

The table fell silent. It was a deeper silence than the normal quiet of “I’m focused on understanding Sebastian.”

“Someone tripped the int sec — internal security, the blow itself up bit, on a mine core, my last proper run. Then they froze instead of calling it or trying to cancel the main charge. Got hit in the eye when I was looking to see if we all made it.”

Sebastian took many quick sips of his beer. “We didn’t. Four of us — Adrian, Adam, Bogdan, Bumper — that is, Michael — got  blown up right there, Pavel — not you — died on the way out, our Robert in the hospital. Michael — other one, leader — was fine, made sure we got out, but ran in front of a truck. Thought it was all his fault.

“Now it’s me and Janek at the shelter and … Philip moved back in with his family far away, I think. Lost a leg.”

Sebastian buried his snout in what was left of his beer. “Fuck, I miss them. Worked together years, no problems, very careful, good guys. One fuckup, though, and … yeah.”

Silence fell over the table. “Shit, sorry, shouldn’t have asked,” Anna said. “Should’ve realized it’d be bad.”

“I chose to answer,” Sebastian said. “Now you know.”

Sylvia stood, knelt down, and gave Sebastian a hug. Her hand started down Sebastian’s back, but she stopped herself. “Right, no petting.”

“G\[o\],” Sebastian howled, forgetting he shouldn’t yell in here, his speech unsteady. “Know you know I’m a person.” He let Sylvia pet him for a while, then she stood back up. *Might want to let a few people do that,* Sebastian thought. *If I trust them.*

Amir drained his soda and mumbled something in a language Sebastian didn’t understand. Maybe a prayer.

Pavel had a practical solution to this. “Get that man,”  he said, pointing down at Sebastian, “another beer.”

When the beer arrived, Pavel took his glass and took up a spot on the floor next to his canine colleague. He clanked it against the bowl of beer. “To all your friends and mine who can’t be here.”

Sebastian leaned against Pavel and licked his face.

Soon after that, Anna took another swing at asking Sebastian personal questions. “Could you have moved in with your family, Seb?”

“They wanted the pension, not the problem,” Sebastian said. “Is what is.” He didn’t elaborate, and his friends didn’t press.

Sebastian didn’t say much as he made his way through about a fourth of the beer. Then, he noticed he was feeling unsteady, which took some effort on four legs.

He shuffled himself into readiness to stumble away. “All clear,” he said, trying to keep it crisp and understandable while not being fit to do so, “outw’d … march.”

Pavel saw how Sebastian wasn’t holding up from his seat on the floor. “You’re way too drunk to march anywhere — we’re taking you home.” He looked up. “Anyone know where the fuzzbutt lives?”

“The city animal shelter all the way west,” Anna said before Sebastian had had a chance to start responding.

“I can do that,” Amir offered. “Maybe drop you off last, Seb?”

Pavel and Anna insisted on sticking by Sebastian, while Sylvia needed to be dropped off at home. She needed to be up early to get the bus back to her car.

Sebastian had laid down while the travel plans were being arranged. “I can slip here,” he insisted, merging two e’s into an i.

Pavel gave him a light nudge with his foot. “Up you get, soldier. Let’s get you home.”

Sebastian got up reluctantly. When he got to Amir’s car, he needed a hand getting into the trunk.

When he and his colleagues had reached the shelter, it took Sebastian three tries to open the dog door in the front gate.

Once Sebastian was inside, the rest of his team heard some thuds and canine voices they struggled to make out (“... where …” “door”) before the door unlocked with a *BZZT*.

Everyone filed in. “Hey, Janek,” Anna said, yawning.

“Hi,” Janek said before turning back to Sebastian. “You had me worried. Thought you’d grown tire tracks or something. Didn’t think to have them call me?”

“Sorry,” Sebastian said. “Didn’t … so late.”

“Let’s get Seb to bed, yeah?” Pavel suggested. “At least it’s his weekend tomorrow.”

Janek yipped a follow-me at Sebastian and emphasized with it a “g\[o\].”

“aw awwo,” Sebastian mumbled. It might have been an attempt at “m\[oving\].”

Janek led the group down some corridors Anna (let alone Pavel and Sylvia) hadn’t been down before. Anna had been to the shelter a few times now, but hadn’t ever gone to see where Sebastian lived.

The shelter was quieter than it had been on those previous visits. The non-human residents’ barking and meowing wasn’t too loud at this time of night, and many of the veterans were asleep, though not all of them quietly.

Anna wasn’t sure what she’d expected to see when they stopped at a door with Sebastian’s name on it, but a double-wide dog kennel wasn’t it.

By dog standards, Sebastian’s living space was wonderful. He had a large comfortable bed *and* a nest of blankets and pillows, a stash of food (and bowls that the staff filled mostly on schedule), and a water bowl that refilled itself (plumbing to keep it always topped up didn’t make the budget, unfortunately). He could go in and out at will. He had shelves of personal effects (there weren’t many, but the paperwork had to go somewhere, and, unlike with typical dogs, couldn’t be stored on the door), puzzles, and toys. His part of a long, thin window high up on the ground  had a shade whose cord went all the way to the floor. Most importantly, he had call buttons to reach reception (for emergencies or otherwise) and a small book of words to point at if needed.

By human standards, well, this wing of the shelter had broken the city record for code variances, even starting from the rules for dormitories. If anything were to go wrong with the army wing, it wouldn’t take long for a swarm of tenants’ rights lawyers to encircle the place looking for clients.

Sebastian unlocked his door and stumbled through it. Janek followed him in and helped him unbuckle his vest and place it on the appropriate shelf. Sebastian climbed into bed and sprawled out.

“Water,” Janek said.

Sebastian whined.

“Water. You’ll thank me tomorrow.”

Sebastian climbed out of bed, drank some water, and got right back into bed, turning around a few times before settling in.

Janek left Sebastian’s room so he could get some sleep.

Anna looked at Sebastian’s room. “You live like this?” she asked Sebastian.

She was quite sure that the tired bark she got as an answer was a sarcastic yes.

“This makes my college dorm look like a fancy hotel.”

Sebastian didn’t reply to that.

“Like, where’s the bathroom, anyway? I could use one.”

Janek took her back down the hall and showed her a door around a corner. Anna walked in and walked right back again. The room she’d been taken to had the wall opposite the door occupied by a trough in the ground that had individually flushable, partitioned-off sections, which had more than control one button mounted to the floor for reasons Anna wasn’t clear on from immediate inspection. Stalls containing what looked to be showers and fur dryers further off to the left. The wall nearest the door, immediately to the right, contained low sinks.

Janek remembered who he was escorting. “Right, two-legged bathroom is right by the front lobby,” he said, turning to take Anna there. “I’ll say, those are the best bathrooms I’ve had since the swap. Beat the hell out of kicking dirt and river baths, or dealing with the normal ones.”

While Anna was occupied, Pavel leaned down and whispered a few questions into Janek’s ear.

Janek answered his questions with tail wagging and a smirk on his face. He knew Sebastian was going to love the plan he’d suggested for next week, even though parts of it would lead to Sebastian getting some friendly revenge on him.

A week later, a few hours into day shift, Pavel put the plan Janek had helped him formulate into action.

“Hey, Sebastian, do me a favor real quick?” he asked.

Sebastian turned his head to look at him. “Q\[uery\].”

“Track,” Pavel commanded, pointing at a small pile of crumbs on the floor. Sebastian gave them a sniff and caught a scent of familiar food and a direction. He decided not to question the command — he liked finding things, and he didn’t mind an excuse to break his routine. He kept picking up the trail of bits of whatever he was following as it wound through the noisy scent-scape of an airport terminal. He wound his way into the employee area and down some hallways, nose to the floor and tail up.

Sebastian’s world narrowed as he focused on the traces of scents around him, as it often did. He found himself lost on his task as he went into a room with the lights off. He registered that several of his coworkers were there and that the lights had clicked on, but that paled in the recognition of the source of what he was tracking, which was right *there\!* His tail wagged as he hurried forward.

Sebastian made contact with the edge of the object nose first, which wasn’t how he’d been taught to respond to finding something, but he’d gotten carried away. Again.

His first impression of the thing was that it was creamy and on his face.

“Happy birthday, Sebastian\!”

Sebastian looked up to see the small party that had been thrown for him. It wasn’t everyone — they wouldn’t have fit — but it was all the people he worked and talked with most often, including everyone he’d gotten drunk with a week ago.

The dog licked the icing off his nose. He supposed he shouldn’t be surprised by this — he’d announced cake in the break room this morning — but he hadn’t even *considered* that it was for him.

“You didn’t need to —”

“Since when have we not gotten everyone cake in the break room on their birthday?” Anna asked.

*She’s got a point,* Sebastian thought. *And I guess it means I’m obviously an everyone, which … ok. That’s good. Yes.*

“How’d you know it was my birthday?”

“Saw it on your ID,” Pavel explained.

Sebastian took a bite of his cake, and then abandoned propriety to wolf the cake down. While his initial assault was quick, it left a lot of crumbs to chase down, which he happily saw to.

“How’d you know to do maple and sausage?” he asked, not even trying to stop his tail from hitting Piotr in the leg.

“I have my sources,” Pavel said cryptically.

Sylvia knelt down next to Sebastian. “We couldn’t think of anything to get you, but we thought we’d at least have a small party.” She had a forkful of the smaller slice of cake on her plate. “And I wasn’t expecting sausage cake to work, but I’m liking it.”

Amir leaned down, tilting his plate towards his furrier coworker. “Not a fan, personally. I’ll stick to cake cake. Want mine, Seb?”

Sebastian absolutely did.

Conversations picked back up now that people weren’t trying to pay attention to what Sebastian was saying, which was fine by him. He was busy making sure people hadn’t left any potential biological hazards (that is to say, more cake) on the floor.

When he’d made it to the far side of the room, Pavel reached down and put a hand on Sebastian’s back. Sebastian paused.

“Just wanted to let you know that that was some absolutely textbook tracking there earlier, Sniffer.”

Sebastian froze as he processed the implications. *I’m going to have to get back at Janek for this.* “When you see y\[ou\]r source,” he replied, “tell them they slept through tick check again.”

Pavel chuckled. “Hey, Anna, Seb told me to tell you you slept through tick check again. Know what that’s about?”

Sebastian burst into laughter as best he could with his canine anatomy. *Guess I’m really one of them now,* he concluded, *seeing as the pranks have already started.*

“Will untie your shoes,” Sebastian threatened Pavel with his tongue out and tail wagging.

Sebastian walked around, listening to people and thinking about what happened. *Maybe complaining more is working,* he decided. *Maybe I have more friends now. Maybe they’re not just seeing a dog.*

*Maybe I should  … worth a try.*

Sebastian went to stand by the big table in the middle of the room. He barked a few times to get everyone’s attention.

“Thank you for this,” he announced. “For being friends.”

He took a deep breath. “I trust you here to pet me and still know I’m not a dog. Just sometimes. Not on shift.” He hadn’t extended this offer to anyone since he’d left the Army, where he’d only trusted a few handlers to not get too mixed up about what he was.

His coworkers (new friends, now) hesitantly approached him and, first individually and then as a rotating group, took him up on his offer.

Sebastian laid down on his side, legs spread out, and smiled. “Th — n\[o\] n\[o\]\!”

The hands stopped.

“N\[o\] belly rubs,” he clarified. He didn’t trust his new friends that much yet.

“Sorry,” Anna said.

“Couldn’t know,” Sebastian assured her.

He received a few more pets while he was saying that, and then the crowd dispersed. Sebastian picked himself up and left with a “m\[oving\] cargo.”

He needed to go process the realization he’d found another pack in peace.

Sebastian knew he needed that space. He wasn’t always that good at accepting things.

**Epilogue:**  Around a month later, Anna came to Sebastian with a proposition.

“So, I was thinking, ever since I saw where you’re living at the animal shelter, that you might want somewhere less … like that. I’m not saying we should do anything super quickly, but would you like to come by my place and see what you think? I can make dinner.”

Sebastian considered this. “Tomorrow? Don’t want to scare Janek.”

“Tomorrow works, yeah,” Anna agreed.

That was how Sebastian found himself entering a house for the first time in many months.

Anna gave him a tour of the place. It was nice — certainly nicer than the house he’d grown up in. The ground floor was an open space that blended the living room and kitchen, and there were two bedrooms upstairs, one of which wasn’t being used for much and could, hypothetically, be his. There was a modest backyard that had been taken over by any plants that could lay claim on it.

“This place used to be me and my fiance, and then, well, he left for the war and didn’t come back,” Anna told Sebastian after the tour was over. “It’s felt empty ever since, even though I’ve been ignoring it for a while.”

“I’m sorry to hear that,” Sebastian said. “Would hug but,” he held up a front paw.

Anna knelt down to give him a hug instead.

Sebastian soon took up a position on the couch, where he considered the idea of what Anna was offering. He looked around and thought about what he’d seen here, and came to a realization.

As he’d thought, he’d watched Anna in the kitchen. She seemed to be enjoying putting something together for the two of them, and she talked to Sebastian as she worked. “It’s been a while since I’ve cooked for someone,” she said. “Forgot how much I liked it.”

He decided to let the thought wait until after dinner.

Anna had done a good job with the food — she’d put together a beef stew that was easy to eat with a snout and tasted richly spiced despite the need to dodge the long list of things Sebastian couldn’t have since becoming a canine.

Sebastian took his time eating, wanting to savor the effort his friend had put into this meal and wanting to put off the conversation he knew he needed to have just a while longer.

He didn’t want to upset Anna, especially with how generous she was being. He didn’t want to lose someone in the group of friends he’d only just made.

But … he was supposed to complain more, and that probably meant outside of work too.

“So, how was it?” Anna asked, after Sebastian had made sure the bowl he’d been served in was completely clean.

“Wonderful,” Sebastian said. “Really good. Thank you so much\! Been a while since had food like this.”

“It wasn’t any trouble,” Anna insisted.

Nothing was said next, so Sebastian decided to approach the topic.

“Re me moving in,” he said, “did you plan to add extra handles or flaps to all the doors?”

Anna considered this. “Hadn’t really thought about it.”

“What about second light switches for me? The thing that was too expensive at work.”

“I … is that important? I could just get those.”

Those were the answers Sebastian had been worried about. “Can I be honest?” he asked. “Scared I’ll upset you and we won’t be friends.”

Anna braced herself for bad news. “Go on. I know this is a lot.”

“Don’t think you know what me moving here would be,” Sebastian said. “What I’d need to not be stuck here, not trapped here, not depending on you too much in a way I don’t want. What you’d have to do because I can’t do it also … make food, call people, that stuff. Worried you didn’t think about all this.

“Not saying never, but … don’t like the idea. And … I like the other mine-dogs. Like living with them. We get each other. We look out for each other. Don’t want to lose that.

“Don’t want to lose work either. Sorry for this. I know you care.”

Anna took a moment to make sure she didn’t say anything too silly or that would hurt Sebastian’s feelings. “You’re right,” she said. “I didn’t think this through. I just wanted to help.”

“I know,” Sebastian said. “I can visit sometimes,” he offered. “Try more food.”

“I’d like that,” Anna said. “… yeah, I’d like that.”

Sebastian went to stand by the door. “Should go home,” he said. “Don’t want to worry squad.” Then, he realized he should, perhaps, not just try to leave. “Thank you for not being mad.”

Anna started putting her shoes back on, got her keys, and went to open the door. “Not sure why you thought I’d be mad at you for saying that,” she said. “You were right. I didn’t think this through.”

The car ride back to Sebastian’s house was nearly silent. Neither Anna or Sebastian had much they wanted to say.

They reached Sebastian’s home. and the dog said “See you later\!” as he left, tail wagging.

When he told the other mine-dogs how his evening had gone, Sebastian got some compliments on how he’d handled everything, often delivered in the form of jokes about how the supply division must have finally gotten his spine in.

As the soldier in the body of a dog watched some TV to unwind, he realized that he had two groups of friends now, and was glad to have them both.

Having all these people who cared about him, whether they had two legs or four, was a lot for Sebastian to accept.

But Sebastian had always been good at accepting things.
