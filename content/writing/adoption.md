A man was petting Scout 62 in a park.

Scout had forked some months ago, giving himself a dog’s body. Four legs, paws, a tail he could wag — the whole nine yards. His down-tree was Tomash, an anthropomorphic dog who’d always been dogged by the desire to relax (or escape) by truly being an animal. Scout 62, like the rest of his pack-mates, were the result of him deciding to make that dream real and many tries at getting the canine mindset right.

When Scout came into being, he’d held a plan in mind. He would wander the myriad sims of the System until he found a spot to settle down in, from which he’d take a name. While he did that, he’d settle into being just a dog. He had the memories of previous Scouts who’d merged down and the advice of the still-living pack, so this wasn’t as hard as it used to be.

Scout, like his down-tree, wanted to be helpful and useful, even as a dog. Many of his pack-mates did this by keeping watch on a spot so they could fetch their elder if someone needed Tomash’s systech powers or knowledge to help them.

This Scout instead found himself drawn back to the park.

He and the man who was currently gently scratching Scout’s forehead had kept running into each other here. Scout 62 liked the man — he was kind and the sleepy human intelligence that Scout hadn’t fully shaken enjoyed their one-sided conversations. It helped that he knew almost, but not entirely, nothing about the branch of math the man had devoted his time to.

The man was lonely. He’d said as much during some of their many meetings. Scout could tell that the man was troubled and sad a lot, and that being around him helped. He liked being helpful.

These realizations and memories slowly drifted through Scout 62’s brain as the man and the dog existed with each other.

The man stopped scratching and sighed. “I wish I could take you home, Scout,” he said. “I miss having a dog, and you’re a nice one. I’ve tried some of the pet dog constructs and none were quite right. … You’re probably not rigged for a house, though.”

Scout had an idea\! He sent a sensorium message to the man, since his mouth wasn’t suited for speaking. “*You can do that\! I want that\! I think.”*

The man looked around. “Who said that?”

“*Me,*” Scout said, holding up a paw.

The man looked down at Scout. “*You?”* he replied. “*You’re … you can talk?*”

“*I can, like this\! Dogs don’t, though. So only if important.*”

The man thought about this. “*So … you’re a person. Who became a dog and acts like one.*”

“*Yes, but … I’m a dog.*”

The man looked down at Scout, studying him. “*I have* so *many questions.*”

Scout didn’t say anything. If the man had questions, he could ask them. Scout was going to be a dog about it.

“*Why?*”

Scout’s tail wagged. “*I don’t have to think about people stuff\! I’m just a dog\! It’s fun\!*”

“*Doesn’t it get boring?”* the man asked.

“*If it’s too boring, I can go home. Quit.*”

The man nodded. “*So ... you just live in this park?*”

“*Been around. Looking for my spot.*”

The man reached down to pet Scout. “*You said I can take you home. You … want to move in? I … that sounds nice.*”

Scout got to his feet and ran around the bench the man was sitting on to burn away some excitement. He stood in front of the man, tail wagging. “*Yes\! I think you’re my spot.*”

The man scratched his head. “*Why me?*”

“*You’re fun and nice and your words are good to listen to and I like you and want you to be happy\!*” Scout said, his words nearly matching his tail for frantic excited speed.

The man leaned down and ruffled Scout’s fur all over. “*You’re adorable. Have I told you that? Just the cutest dog.*”

Scout didn’t say anything and leaned into the pets.

“*Right. Dog. Dogs don’t talk.*”

Scout barked.

“*... Fuck it. I can try adopting you. Having you as a roommate. Whichever —*”

Scout cut the thought off by scampering off for more zooming around. He didn’t get far before the man sent “*Scout. C’mere\! Here boy\!”*

Scout spun back around and ran back to the man.

“*Sit.*”

Scout sat. He was an obedient dog. A good dog. Mostly.

“*Good boy,”* the man said. “*Good to know I won’t have to teach you everything.*”

Scout stayed put.

“*Right, so … how far do you want to take this? You want to go on walks and eat kibble and everything?*” the man asked.

“*I’m a dog,”* Scout replied. “*If I or you need not-dog, we can talk. But I’m a dog. I’m your dog. While we want. Your good boy.*”

The man smiled. “*I can work with that. No chewing up my stuff, though.*”

Scout held up a paw to shake on it. “*Deal.*”

The man shook the paw. Then, he had a realization. “*Just realized I never asked … is this a weird sex thing?”*

“*No sex,*” Scout answered firmly. “*But not* not *a kink thing.*”

“Hm?” the man asked.

“*Not being people fun many ways.*” That felt like explanation enough to Scout.

“*Mm-hm. So, uh, want to get going? See your new home?*”

At that, Scout stood back up, but resisted the urge to scamper around again. The man stood up to gather his things.

As the man was nearly finished packing, Scout realized he had a question. “*What’s your name?*” he asked. “*You’re my human or Leader or Master, but … need to change tag. Show spot, not puppy number.*”

“*It never came up, did it?”* the man replied.

Scout said nothing. Dogs tried to avoid answering that sort of question, and he was a dog.

“*Skelly,*” the man said.

Scout didn’t visibly react, and seemed to be staring at something in the distance.

“*Feed name, y’know? Was so used to people calling me that that I kept it when I uploaded.”*

Scout sat. “*Done. Good?”*

The man crouched down to get a good look at Scout’s collar tag. It, and his System directory info, had been updated with a new name: Scout With Skelly.

“*Smart dog\! … Uh, anything else?*” Skelly asked.

Scout looked around. “*Can we … no thinking words at each other unless need to talk like people?*”

“Well, here’s the place,” he proclaimed, opening the front door. “I hope you’ll like it here.”

Once he had removed the temptation, he looked over and saw Scout unable to decide between looking guilty and quite satisfied with his heist. Skelly burst out laughing, “You really *are* a dog\!”

Scout didn’t have anything to say to that. He was a dog, as much as he could be, after all.

Skelly soon wrapped up the tour and sat down at his desk. Scout flopped onto his side nearby, and was quite happy to have Leader reach down and pat him now and again.

Scout did his best not to display an opinion. He did want to be involved in the shopping. On the other paw,  having someone other than his down-tree (so, in some sense, himself) picking out the dog gear and surprising him with it would be more appropriate to the role he was trying to inhabit. But, more importantly, this wasn't a question he was supposed to understand. Dogs don't really understand their people.

Skelly waited for a reaction. “*So, do you want to go to the pet store with me?*” he asked, remembering that more serious communication with his dog (roommate, sort of?) was meant to be done via sensorium message.

“*You decide*,” was all Scout would say on the matter.

“Well, if I take the dog thing seriously, I don't entirely know how you behave on leash in public yet, and a pet store may not be the best place to find out, even though you seemed fine in the park. So I should probably leave you here. But also, I don't think I should leave you with access to the entire house in case you get into something. Hm … right, that works, and it'll let us double-check how deeply you want to do the dog thing.”

Skelly picked up the leash he'd left attached to Scout. Scout followed him as he was led to the house’s smaller bathroom. Skelly let go of the leash and stepped out of the room backwards, closing the door quickly so the dog wouldn't follow him. “See you soon, Scout\!” he called through the door, his voice muffled.

Scout barked and whined a few times, as was appropriate for being trapped without Leader, since he coild still hear his footsteps as he left the house.

Then, once he'd heard the front door close and he couldn't hear Leader anymore, Scout laid down on the floor mat to wait.

It wasn't the most comfortable wait, but that was to be expected, given his sudden adoption. He laid in the small room and did his best to let time pass by in a haze of half-asleep existence. This was a very boring thing, but he knew it was part of what he signed up for. Being a dog like this wasn't all fun, games, and pets, sometimes it was napping in a bathroom and waiting for his person to come back because he wasn't fully trusted to stay home alone.

He could remember many such moments of stillness in his life and the lives of other Scouts. They were part of why he'd name himself into a dog: these times where all he had to do — all he could do — was exist and be nothing more than an animal. No responsibilities, few decisions, and no worries (except, perhaps, the little canine fear that his new packmate would never return). It was a nothing much happening he could enjoy for a while yet.

Meanwhile, Skelly wandered through a pet store. He didn't need advice, so this was mainly a matter of grabbing whatever he needed from the shelves or noting down he could summon it when he got home. He had a lot of choices, especially since the lines between pet supplies, kink gear, and adaptive items for furries was thinner sys-side.

His biggest problem was that he didn't quite know what Scout liked. Skelly’s collection of kibble samples, beds, blankets, chew toys, treats, and so on kept growing, making him glad he'd brought a bag of holding. The sheer volume of choices he was making made him realize what Scout was getting away from by being a dog in a park — no, his dog, now — and why he hadn't wanted to decide if he was coming on this trip. He didn't find himself wanting to run away the same way, though. He'd get bored too quickly.

Eventually, Skelly idly wondered how long he'd been looking around. The System told him, and he responded by quickening his steps and wrapping up his shopping so he could get home. Had it really been that long?

Scout’s head snapped up when he heard the front door opening and footsteps in the distance. He stood back up and stretched, hoping he'd be let out soon.

He didn't have to wait long. Skelly set his bag down and went right to where he'd left his new dog. He opened the door, and was greeted by Scout jumping on him.

He reached down to pet Scout. “Sorry I took so long. Got distracted. I hope you're OK.”

Scout just seemed happy to have his friend back.

Skelly walked Scout to the kitchen, where he started unpacking his bag. He stated with what he imagined Scout would say was his most important purchase: food. Skelly set up a water bowl and an empty food bowl.

Then, he started putting small samples of the various dog foods he'd found in Scout's bowl. Scout made sure to give each one a good sniff before chowing down, and even, in a case of not entirely dog-like behavior, used his water to cleanse his palette between samples. None of them were what he'd have picked, but … he was a dog, the choice wasn't his, and he liked that.

Skelly came away from the exercise with a decent guess about what Scout had liked best and was about to ask him to confirm before he remembered that he'd probably be told to decide again. “*You'd tell me if you really didn't like the food, Scout, right?*” he asked.

“*Yes*,” his dog replied.

So, Skelly made a bag of what was Scout's food (for now) appear near him. The System picked up the rest of his intent, though, and made a serving of that food appear directly in Scout's bowl.

Scout set aside the urge to give Skelly advice on how to avoid this sort of thing, as he might have were he still a person, and dove face first into his dinner, crunching up a storm.

He was so busy eating that he didn't even notice his man changing his bowls to say “Scout” on them.

After he'd eaten, Scout followed Leader around as he buried himself unpacking all the dog stuff. Scout gave the beds and other soft things tentative tries, but he was too full of energy to nap. He was disappointed to see most of the toys getting put away for later, though he did enjoy spending time finding the squeaker in a little plush thing while Leader worked around him.

Finally, Leader took up Scout's leash again and … walkwalkwalkWALK\!\!\! … led him outside to explore his neighborhood. Scout spend some of  his time giving his surroundings a good sniff so he'd know where home was and what other creatures (he recognized some of the common construct scent marks) were nearby and other parts zooming around as far his leash would go. He and his pack had been on walks before — his down-tree and some of his friends had taken him — but they’d usually been more prearranged than this.

Therefore, his first walk around the neighborhood saw him being bundle of excitement and tail wags balanced precariously against a desire to explore and make the most of his time outside, which was much more limited than it had been this morning. Being taken around an unfamiliar place on a leash with no possible idea of where he was going was also taking him even deeper into being a pet, adding a more complex set of joys to his first time out as the man’s — no, Leader's, perhaps sometimes Skelly’s — pet dog. For his part, Skelly let Scout have his excitement and smiled as the dog he'd both known for a few months and hadn't known until today walked with him.

After the walk, Leader sat down to do people stuff and didn't pet Scout too much. Eventually, Scout decided to go explore the house on his own, and Leader didn't stop him. This house was interesting, from a dog's-eye view. He discovered that the couch was quite comfortable, and hoped Leader wouldn't mind him laying on it in the future. He found a crate that he hadn't seen before, too, and decided that he'd need to be properly acclimatized to it (that is, bribed with treats).

Eventually, Scout returned to curl up near his human and see what would happen next. Some time later, said human got up and began to do things that Scout's human mind recognized as getting ready for bed. Scout tailed him when he could, but wasn't allowed in the bathroom or bedroom, much to his disappointment.

“Goodnight, Scout,” Leader called out through his closed door. “I've left out a bunch of beds for you, see which one you like.”

Scout With Skelly continued to love being with Skelly. Being a pet dog — someone/something that existed to give and receive affection and companionship — suited who he’d become.

He and Leader didn’t take long to let their relationship settle into the shape they both wanted. Scout was a dog. Skelly had a dog. That was how the world worked. The fact that there was a person somewhere in Scout’s sily little head did come up occasionally, but they both kept to their roles as best they could. Scout learned how Leader liked him to behave and how he liked to receive affection from his pet, and Skelly learned what his dog liked — where he wanted to be scratched, what treats were his favorite, what little novelties and games he needed to stave off boredom (and thus a tendency to act up), and what parts of his mathematical work the human mind underneath Scout seemed most interested in.

Now, over nine years after his adoption, Scout could feel himself growing restless. No, not quite restless. Not quite bored either. The sense that he was full — not of food but of experience. He’d had wonderful times with his Leader, been the good dog (and, sometimes, bad dog) he’d wanted to be … but he could tell this couldn’t be his forever. There was only so much of nothing much happening he could take.

He’d had some of these feelings before. He’d talked about hem with Leader, and they’d tweaked what his life was like in response, but now … he knew that there wasn’t anything for it.

Scout didn’t want to leave right away, and … just didn’t want to be the one to bring up departure, to be the one who’d take the first step. Not out of the blue.

Instead, Scout let himself grow old. He didn’t age years in a night, but he let the signs build up, wanting to hint at what he was thinking of doing so it wouldn’t be a shock. He was a clever dog, after all, and he didn’t want to hurt Leader more than he knew he would.

One day, Scout was up on the couch with Leader, when he got a message. “*I noticed you’re looking older. Is that just something that’s happening or is there something wrong?*”

Scout yelped in surprise, then looked up at Leader. “*I … I’m sorry …,”* Scout pressed himself against his human, “*Been thinking. While. That … can’t be a pet anymore. “*I’m sorry, Skelly,*” Scout said. He didn’t use Leader’s name often when talking to him.

That was enough to make Skelly cry.

Scout couldn’t think of anything to do but be a comforting weight, and that’s what he was.

A week later, it was time.

Skelly and Tomash settled in well. There were bumps in the road, as there usually are, but Tomash had just under a decade of living with Skelly, while Skelly had spent enough time around Scout to guess at how Tomash liked things. It wasn’t exactly having lived together for years already, but it was close.

Yet, no amount of trying each other’s cooking for the first time or fiddling with the closet layouts could put off the reason Tomash, or perhaps Scout\#anthro, had come into being forever.

“*Tomorrow?*” Scout asked Leader and people-him. “*Feels like time.*”

Skelly sighed. “Has to be one of these days. Wish it didn’t have to be. I’ll … miss you. I’ll miss having a dog.”

“*Won’t be all gone,*” Scout said. He went to provide comfort the way he could — comforting pressure and some licks.

“Won’t be the same,” Skelly replied. “I like you as a person, but it’s different.”

“I needed different,” Tomash added. “Maybe someday … I became Scout before. But don’t know when. Don’t know if.”

“I’m not expecting anything,” Skelly said. “Good to know, though.”

He looked down at his dog. “*Tomorrow, then. Let me know what you want to end on.*”

The next day, Scout had a good time. Fetch in the park where he and Leader had met, a long walk, and even samples of some of the food he’d been itching to try ever since he’d smelled them.

Then he curled up on his bed. “*Thank you. I loved being your dog. I love you.*” he said.

Skelly knelt down and put a hand on Scout’s side. “I love you too. You were a great dog, Scout. A very good dog. Thank you so much.”

Tomash forked so that he’d stick around then merged with Scout so his first week as a person would flow down-tree.

Scout blinked. He took in the memories.

He sighed, closed his eyes, and quit.

Skelly’s hand now rested on empty space.

No one said anything. No one knew what to say.

Skelly was the first to break the silence. “Hey. Tomash? Sorry for ruining the moment, but, I forgot to ask — could we still go on walks? I like having the excuse to go outside.”

Tomash was relieved that the silence was broken. “Sure,” he said. He made a larger version of Scout’s collar, one sized for his neck, appear in his hand, and offered it to Skelly.

“What’s this for?” Skelly asked.

“Walking me,” Tomash said. “Not every time, but, well ..”

“This one of the dog instincts you’ve still got running around? You don’t need a leash now, you know?”

Tomash shook his head. “I know I don’t, but … remember what you asked right before you brought me home?”

Skelly thought back to that conversation. “What about it?”

“Like I said, being a dog isn’t *not* a kink thing.”

Skelly pulled Tomash into a hug. “Silly rascal,” he said. “Just like you’ve always been.”

Then, on impulse, he buckled the collar around Tomash’s neck. “It’s on good?”

“Yep,” Tomash said.

Skelly took Scout’s old leash and clipped it on. “Fuck it. Let’s go to the park before my brain catches up with me. C’mon, boy\!”

Tomash followed his partner — still Leader, sometimes, like he’d realized he wanted, over the last week — out the door for his walk, tail wagging.

He looked back at the house.

As a dog, he’d loved it here.

As a person again, he still did.
