"We go now to an interview with I Remember The Rattle Of Dry Grass of
the Ode clade, who has experience as a System engineer sys-side and has
been involved in efforts to catalogue the damage from the Century
Bombing," said a news anchor, as the screen cut to a short stocky woman
with curly black hair and a somber expression who stood in a grassy
field, alone. The chevron at the bottom of the screen read "8.9 to 23
billion dead in Century Bombing of the System"

"Good afternoon, Ms. Dry Grass," said the softer voice of her
interviewer.

"Good morning, madam," the woman replied.

"Before we begin, you have my condolences on the losses you have
suffered as a result of this terrible crime."

"Thank you," Dry Grass replied, doing her best to hold back the ripple
of emotions across her face. "It has been a difficult time for us, and
so, I work. Perhaps we will uncover a miracle."

The interviewer paused.

"Now, you wanted to discuss how many have been lost," Dry Grass
continued.

"Ah, yes. Do you know, Ms. Dry Grass, when we'll have a more precise
estimate of the number of how many people are unrecoverable on the
System? Has there been any further progress on recovery efforts?"

"You will have no better estimate," Dry Grass stated.

"Could you expand on that, please?"

"Around 9 billion clades are dead, around 23 billion instances are dead.
No other count is possible."

This wasn't an answer the interviewer was prepared for, and her research
on the System slipped out of her head. "Clades? Instances?"

"An instance is a mind running within the System. A clade is the group
of instances, perhaps only one, who trace themselves back to one
uploaded individual, the root instance."

"So, if I've understood you correctly, you're having trouble estimating
because of forks?" the interviewer asked, hoping she was headed in the
right direction.

"You could say that," Dry Grass said.

Suddenly, a second copy of her appeared to her left, waved to the
camera, and disappeared.

"That was a fork of me," Dry Grass continued. "For a few seconds, we
stood in different places and did different things. We can, I think,
agree that a few seconds of differing experience did not make my fork a
different person.

"But what if she went her own way? What if we separated to think
different thoughts? Read different books? Meet different people? Take
different names? When can we be said to be different people, madam?"

"I ... that's a complex philosophical question with no clear answer,"
the interviewer said.

Dry Grass nodded. "Yes. No one has found individuation under a
microscope. But, as a practical matter, we could ask instances if they
would consider themselves a different person from some other cocladist
of theirs, if we sought to have more correct tallies of the damage. Even
then, we would miss those who are in the process of becoming themselves.

"But we cannot ask, madam. Those we would ask are unrecoverable. Lost.
Dead. This is why all we can know, with any certainty, are deaths by
clade and by instance. Any number between them would be a guess woven of
shreds of memory. A web of false certainty that masks the true scale of
our lack and loss."

"I ... see," the interviewer responded. "Thank you for your time, Ms.
Dry Grass, and our condolences again."

"Thank you," Dry Grass said. "Goodbye, madam," This fork of Dry Grass
nodded and quit out, cutting the AVEC connection.
