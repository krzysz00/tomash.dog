Route 227 rarely got close to busy, and ‘packed’ was pretty much out of
the question. There were several factors working against it. For one, it
was a rural bus service. It lazily wound its way up and down the narrow
highway heading east from New Brookford, often pulling off to stop by
some particular village or even an individual restaurant. The bus wasn’t
frequent, but it made up for it by sticking to its schedule, as there
wasn’t much traffic to get stuck in.

The buses that plied this route weren’t great for attracting new riders
either. They rattled, the heat was too hot in the winter and the AC was
too cold in the summer, the fabric on the seats had worn out over the
decades, and there was often something squeaky. One could say that the
vehicles had character if one wanted to put a positive spin on things.
This didn’t tend to attract new riders.

Finally, this was a bus route on the System, where it wasn’t too hard to
circumvent the realities of distance — or create a car — if one needed
to.

All this meant that route 227 was mainly frequented by a dusting of
regulars. Even though they didn’t need to, there were folks who liked
living out in the country and taking the bus into town for errands,
visiting friends, the work they’d chosen for themselves, or many other
reasons one might head into town.

The regulars had gotten to know each other over the decades or, in the
case of some of the original residents, over a century and a half they’d
lived out here. Some of them didn’t talk much, and enjoyed their bus
trips as a good time to read, stare out the window half-asleep, or, in
Jimmy’s case, knit. Others saw the trip as a social occasion, disturbing
the occasional city-dwellers who needed a ride all the way out here.

The low ridership of route 227 suited its riders; it had people, but not
too many people, and that’s how they liked it. They were glad to see new
folks join them and sad to see people move away.

Two years ago, a few folks had stopped showing up unexpectedly. Over the
days following, the people east of New Brookford had learned why.

Those first days of restoration had been quiet and tense, but, as time
passed, things warmed back up and almost seemed normal again, except for
Jimmy not being around to work on his scarves anymore and Maria’s Diner
not being a stop anymore due to a lack of Maria.

The anniversary of those deaths had brought back the quiet, though that
time, it was a somber one, not a tense one, as route 227’s first evening
departure bounced its way onto the highway.

This year, the mood was confused, at first. There was some “Happy
Restoration Day!” as folks got on, but people weren’t sure how, or if,
to celebrate the occasion. Neither traditions nor crops grew all that
quickly, after all.

As the bus left the city behind, Cory (the apple farmer, not the tall
one who spelled it with an ‘e’, he had plans with his folks) had an
idea. He wasn’t usually one for this copying-yourself thing, since he’d
always found it confusing, but today, he decided to fork so he could
talk to Jose and Linda at the same time.

What he hadn’t expected was that Jose would send a fork to stand near
Linda too, and that Linda would make a copy of herself who’d stumble her
way to the front of the bus where Cory and Jose had been sitting.

“Got a lot to talk about?” Cory asked the new Linda. “And I love the new
earrings!” (Linda had swapped hers out for bigger hoops during the
fork.)

“I figured that if you’re doing something system-y, I should join in,”
she said. “Makes sense for what the holiday is, no?” Her voice carried
through a lull in the surrounding noise, and then Jose had to reach out
to catch Linda after the bus hit a pothole.

As Cory considered this, the forking began in earnest. Most folks made
at least one copy of themselves, though most of the talkative ones had
several more — one for each person they were thinking of talking to
today proved to be a common pattern.

Soon, the bus was running out of room for its ‘new’ riders. All the
seats filled up quick, and the standing room didn’t take much longer
after that. People were still talking if they felt like it, though they
were increasingly talking to whoever they ended up next to and nudging
the crowd so they weren’t in the strange position of standing next to
themselves.

This wasn’t at all the normal experience on this bus (someone even asked
the construct who usually drove to turn the heat down) but … it was a
holiday. Holidays were often a bit strange.

Soon, the bus reached its first stop, and three Joses and two Carls
(“isn’t it going to be confusing, that both of him were readin’ the same
book?” Linda wondered quietly) jostled their way to the exits with a
chorus of “’scuse me”s, “I’ll see you tomorrow!”s, and “Happy
Restoration Day!”s , where folks stepped off momentarily to let them
through.

Then, route 227 continued on, as those with a view out the back window
saw the Carls merging down but the Joses staying as their separate
selves.

The crowd thinned out as the bus meandered down the road, spoiling its
on-time performance due to all the extra passengers. Near the end of the
line, the remaining passengers had starting to add a few more forks — by
then, they’d decided it should be, oh, let’s say six of them getting off
at the last stop instead of the usual two, and so they did.

No one had planned this, no one had decided that this was what
Restoration Day would look like on route 227, but, from that year on, it
did, especially once the story had properly circulated to everyone who
hadn’t been there.

Sometimes, traditions just spring up, after all.
