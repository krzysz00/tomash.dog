Tomash had, a long time ago, dedicated forks of himself to being dogs.

He was himself an anthropomorphic dog, but these forks, the minds that started out as copies of him and became the Scout pack, went all-in. Four legs, no ability to speak, paws without thumbs, the works.

They went out into the System — all of the Systems — to be dogs. To not worry about calendars and plans and bureaucracy and all that important, yet sometimes overwhelming, people stuff. They’d be dogs. At most, they’d keep an eye out for the incidents and emergencies he could help with

Tomash thought this would be a good experience, and that he’d enjoy the memories of that existence, that simple joy, if and when it came back to him. That he’d have, in the back of his mind, the ghost of having had no thoughts, to balance him out when he was having too many.

He’d been right.

With practice, he’d gotten the hang of becoming a dog, becoming one of these Scouts. His forks had gone out into the world and had, on the whole, a great time\! They’d been helpful, too — Tomash could remember being pinged by a barking Scout letting him know about yet another case of someone flailing about in a panic after turning their gravity off *and* having been that Scout, who’d noticed the issue while napping in a park.

He’d acquired a whole melting pot of dog lives over the decades, from scrounging for food behind coffeeshops to getting adopted to the sheer firehose of everything that was The Party.

He used those memories to create new generations (or perhaps litters) of Scouts every now and again. The System could always do with a couple more dogs.

Tomash’s project had exceeded his expectations, to the betterment of all his selves, both person- and dog-minded.

There was one problem he’d overlooked though: Merge side effects.

Taking in memories of having been someone else changes you. Part of you, going forward, knows what having been your up-tree was like. It can be jarring, having been and not been another person.

Tomash’s reactions became stained by having been the Scout pack.

It could get awkward.

The effects were the worst when he was early in the process of integrating a merge, when the memories and instincts he’d taken in were freshest.

There were several “cafe incidents”. He’d take a seat and order his tea in a shallow dish. That was ordinary enough, for the System, with how many people embraced having a snout or muzzle with their freedom of form.

What wasn’t normal was when he would proceed to set his bowl down on the floor, get down on all fours, and lap at it there. He had years of new-old experiences, recently arrived into his history, his identity, that told him that was how he drank.

Sometimes he’d catch himself before it got to that point. Sometimes he wouldn’t.

Once, someone noticed what was going on, looked down at Tomash, and asked, “You good?”. Tomash looked up from his bowl of tea at that. He realized what he’d done and picked up his drink before standing back up. His tail drooped and his ears folded down.

“Yeah, sorry, thanks, I’m … still taking a merge,” he said. “I’ve got a subclade that really leans into the dog thing and, well, you know how it is.”

He was glad his fur made it hard to see his blush.

The person who’d shaken him out of those borrowed instincts had shrugged, Tomash remembered all too clearly. “No problem,” they’d said. “Glad you’re fine.”

Then there were all the times he’d tried to pick up his pencil with his mouth, forgetting he had hands.

Or leaned down to munch on some bits of food that had fallen on the ground once he’d smelled them.

There’d been surprisingly many times when Tomash had been thinking deeply about something and, before he realized, he’d gotten onto all fours to literally sniff around for the solution in a haze of post-merge confusion.

Some Scouts left him reflexively turning off collisions when he heard a car or a bike for the days it took him to realize that *he* wasn’t that small.

A few of his older merges had given him a temporary dislike of rain as the cold nights outside filtered through him. Fortunately, the newer Scouts tended to turn down the worst impacts of weather on their perception.

By now, Tomash even knew that he should be careful in parks and forests during the first few hours of a merge. He’d had several cases of nearly chasing after critters, and he’d never found a way to make an aborted chase look dignified. Nor had he found a less awkward way to say that someone he hadn’t personally met smelled familiar.

He even had his house prepared with a dog bed in a corner for when he found himself knowing he was meant to sleep there. The collection of toys some other him had played with kept growing, too.

It took days, or sometimes weeks, from a Scout merge for these dramatic effects to quiet down, but, even then, there were still habits that persisted.

Since the Scout project kicked off, Tomash found himself pausing for just a moment before sitting down in a chair or climbing into a bed. Instance-centuries of memories reminded him he wasn’t always allowed on the furniture, even though, as Tomash, he always had been.

Any variation of “Good boy\!” was enough to set his tail wagging these days, though that wasn’t much of a change from before he’d become the Scouts, if he was being honest with himself.

He’d begun keeping kibble and dog treats in his pantry, both for feeding to any Scouts who happened to be around and for snacking on when the mood struck him. He’d developed a taste for the stuff, having been dogs. He’d even talked to sensewrights about making their dog food more grounded, more real, since (looking at the Scouts’ memories with his perspective) he'd seen that much of what had been dreamed into the world didn’t have the details that came from experience.

Tomash had even taken to wearing a collar around his neck, though he kept it invisible much of the time. Being uncollared didn’t feel right anymore.

While Tomash didn’t broadcast his adventures as the Scouts widely, he had friends and fellow systechs who knew about that part of his clade’s life. Many of them weren’t above little pranks either, like creating a ball, throwing it, and calling out “Hey, Tomash, fetch\!” in the middle of a game night.

He’d scrambled out of his seat and was about to take off running before he came back to himself. Once the embarrassment had worn off, he agreed with everyone else: that had been pretty funny. Many such experiences of being a pet — or at least expected to behave in a dog-like way — had a way of sneaking up on him years after those Scouts had come back.

The next week, he got back at the ball-thrower, who was a cat, by setting off her urge to chase a laser pointer.

Some things weren’t effects of his many merged-in lives, though. He’d always been one to walk off his energy, which naturally translated into a case of zooming around as a dog. Chewing on writing implements wasn’t a Scout invention either.

All these quirks, these imprints on the personality born of many decades of having been dogs, could be embarrassing and awkward, but they were worth it.

Tomash knew, as he remembered his own long nights helping people deal with the System and his up-trees’ long days of being themselves, that he wouldn’t change what he’d done if he could.

His tail wagged as he smiled.

That he’d been able to do what he’d done, to be dogs and to have been dogs, was a wonderful thing about the System, and something he wanted to make sure everyone could have if they wanted. It had become part of why he kept helping out and kept up with systech work, over his decades, while the memories he’d merged in kept him from burying joy under a stream of things that had to get done.

He’d made the right choice, he decided. Even with that pesky urge to bark at people to get their attention coming back again.
