There's an animal shelter you'll only find if you truly need to.

It isn't on any maps or in any directories. It doesn't have a nonprofit
registration or an address. It *certainly* doesn't have a website.

Wherever it is when you find it, you'll be sure it wasn't there before.
You would have noticed this strange building with its single story and
faded paint that somehow survived here on Wall Street, in the heart of
the City of London, or, most commonly these days, right between two San
Francisco skyscrapers. You think you would have remembered seeing a
place with such a strange name; what sort of place is “Turing Home for
Formerly Human Dogs”, anyway?

Once you've found it, you'll never have trouble finding it again. It
might take you months or years to go in, especially if you discover that
this animal shelter doesn't seem to exist or that people worry that
you're starting to see things when you try and show it to them. Still,
whether the burning curiosity takes you right away or creeps up on you,
you'll pull open the dusty glass door and step in, heralded by jingling
bells and a creak.

At the front desk, you can find brochures about adopting or getting
adopted. You'll have a sense of which one you should pick up and flip
through while waiting for the person who'd just called out “Sorry, hold
on, I'll be there in a minute” to extricate themselves from their
current task.

Some of the people drawn to this place — perhaps you — are here to get
adopted. If that's you, you're close to cracking under the weight of all
the thoughts you have to have for good. You're probably a lawyer or
stockbroker, or, more often these days, an engineer. You have so many
people to keep up with, decisions to make, projects to plan and create,
pressures and responsibilities... and that's not even counting the
disaster zone that is your life outside of work. You're pretty sure
you're going to burn out (again) if you keep going like this. Maybe
you're already burned out and dragging yourself through all this stress
so that everything doesn't fall apart.

For most people in this position, this means they really should go take
a vacation. Maybe find a therapist or a drinking buddy and pick up a new
hobby. In more extreme cases, this state leads people to, say, move out
to the country to start a cabbage farm or become a monk.

None of that would help you, if you're here to get adopted. No matter
how far you run, you wouldn't be able to escape the weight of simply
being a person. Of being human.

So here, at the Turing Home For Formerly Human Dogs, you can set your
humanity down.

The deal is rather straightforward. You give up your name, your shape,
your hands, your voice, and most of your independence. That is, you
become a dog.

In return, you will have a place here where the staff will get you used
to your new life while they find you a good home. There, you will be
cared for. You will have almost nothing to worry about, no planning to
do, no *goddamn meetings*. You will be loved, and your future will be
securely in the hands of those who love you. You'll have a long and
healthy life with an owner who will know of your past and stay with you
for the rest of your days.

And, should you be worried about how bored you get, you will be useful.
You will have a job of sorts in your new home. You'll be taught what to
do and get plenty of treats for doing it. You'll be a good dog and
you'll know that you've been a good dog (unless you're acting up, of
course), and you'll be able to tell that you're doing something good in
the world. On top of that, you'll have plenty of fun in the large
portion of your time where you can just be a dog.

If this change of lifestyle doesn't call to you, however, you're
probably here to adopt a formerly human dog. People who merely want a
dog won't be offered a chance to do this. Most people who need a dog
will never find this shelter either; many dogs who don't have much of a
human life under their belt are still very intelligent creatures and can
be taught to do many useful things.

But there's something about you that creates an exception. Some
combination of needs that is too complex for a normal dog to help with,
even if there were someone willing to attempt such training. Maybe
things are unpredictable enough that you need a dog who has a firmer
grasp of your goals and who knows something about all these strange
human contraptions. For example, perhaps you need a dog who will
understand what you're saying enough to realize when you're
hallucinating and try to nudge you out of it. Or perhaps you need a dog
who can understand walking directions instead of just memorizing them...
and who also knows what a seizure smells like.

Perhaps knowing your new dog used to be human will make it easier for
you to manage your new relationship too.

If you're worried that this is too much responsibility to take on, that
you wouldn't make a good owner for a formerly human dog, or that you'd
be taking advantage of someone, there’s no need to be. While that’s
ultimately a worry you'd need to answer for yourself, you wouldn't be
offered the chance to adopt at Turing if you weren't worthy of it.

And, if you're worried about all the costs of having a dog, don't be.
The Turing Home for Formerly Human Dogs will get you the food, supplies,
toys and veterinary care your new dog will need for free through all the
years your dog will be at your side.

The shelter has made commitments to their dogs, after all, and it will
keep them.

Now, there is one payment needed, no matter why you are here: lifespan.
You and your owner or dog will have at least thirty years together and
make it to human old age at each other's sides. But, after that, you'll
pass away around the same time as the one your life will be intertwined
with, as it wouldn't be right to leave you (or your partner) alone again
after so long. The extra years you would have had become the shelter's
fee.

Whether you're here to potentially adopt or be adopted, the receptionist
will soon arrive and interrupt your perusal of the information pamphlet
to offer you a tour. If you weren't inclined to take it, you wouldn't be
here, so you go see the place. If you pay close attention to the person
showing you around, you'll notice that something feels strange about
them, as if the mask of normalcy wasn't as metaphorical for them as it
is for you.

The shelter is small. There's room for seven dogs here, but there are
almost always a few empty kennels. As you walk down the hallway, the
dogs will stir. Some might bark a few times to get your attention, but
they understand that harassing the visitors doesn't leave a good
impression. They'll usually all want pets, and you'll be encouraged to
give them if you'd like.

The size, breed, and color of the dogs is all over the place, and your
tour guide cryptically says that all the dogs here end up in the right
shape.

Each kennel has everything a dog would need but not too much else.
There's a soft bed, a bunch of blankets, food and water bowls, and a toy
or two. The floors are bare outside of a square of carpet. “When we get
a dog in here,” the person showing you around explains, “we need to get
them used to slowing down and just being a dog now. It's boring by human
standards and not always the most comfortable, but we've found the
adjustment period's important for successful adoptions.”

The rest of the facility doesn't have too much else. There's a playroom
where the dogs will learn to socialize and a room containing fragments
of a house that the shelter staff (somehow, you've only seen the one)
use to ensure the dogs here know how to behave before they're let loose
into the world. You're shown a room that resembles a vet's office if you
don't look too hard along with the small yard out back.

“We usually take our dogs out into a city to keep them stimulated, but
sometimes we just have to let them out back,” you're told. The yard is
covered in a lush carpet of grasses and flowers, whose vibrancy is in
stark contrast to the run-down feel of the rest of the facility.

Once you've been shown around and given all the time you'd like to play
with the Home's current residents, you're offered adopter or adoptee
paperwork and instructions.

“Take some time,” you're told. “This is the biggest commitment you'll
ever make. If you do decide to go through with this, come back when
you're ready.

“We'll be here for you.”

------------------------------------------------------------------------

Should you, as expected, take the forms home, you'll take that time to
look everything over and consider this. You'll notice that the contract
you've been given is quite thick while trying its best to keep
everything simple: whoever's behind this Home for Formerly Human Dogs
clearly wants to spell out exactly what you're getting into.

When (if, assuming you put much more stock in free will than in fate)
you decide to become a dog or adopt a former human, you'll make your way
back with your application. Someone who's probably the person you met
initially (though you can't be quite sure) will take your application,
confirm that you're sure about this, and give you your next
instructions.

If you're planning to adopt, there isn't much you need to do. As with
any dog, you'll want to make sure your home will be safe for your new
companion. In addition, you'll want to ensure that your dog won't have
easy access to things like computer keyboards or pens while they're
still adjusting to their new home just in case they've still got some of
their stronger human instincts left in their systems. Otherwise, you'll
join the long line of new dog owners who had to repaint after their
animal tried to write on the wall.

If you'll be adopted, the process is more involved. It's difficult to
make a known human fade away entirely, to replace a person with a dog in
the memory of the world. So, you'll need to quit your job, sell things
off or put them in storage, set up your money to be distributed, and,
most importantly, let people know you'll be going away for quite some
time — to start a cabbage farm or become a monk, perhaps. The exact
details don't matter. What's important is that people are expecting you
to disappear. It's much easier to smooth the ripples of someone
tragically not returning from a venture than it is to make a freak
disappearance unremarkable.

In either case, the day your life changes will come sooner than you
expect.

The final step of adopting a dog from the Turing Home for Formerly Human
Dogs is a strange affair. You first meet all the dogs and spend time
with them to get a sense of their personalities while learning a bit
about them from the shelter's manager? volunteer? Is it the same person
each time?

But then, if you think you've found a match, you'll sit down with them
and tell them about yourself. You'll tell them what you need from them
and explain your situation. You'll even tell them some things about
yourself that aren't so serious: maybe you like hiking or make a really
good chicken soup you'd be willing to share on occasion.

As you talk to the dogs, you'll notice one of them — and there will be
one, the shelter honors its commitments to all who work with them — that
just clicks with you. They'll seem more interested, more committed.
You'll notice that this dog isn't just excited to be with someone:
they're excited to be with *you*.

You'll know you've found your match when you feel it. When you mention
you want to take home, the dog will be asked to “growl at them now or
forever hold your peace”. (There was a note, somewhere in all those
instructions, that the option hasn't ever been used, but it needs to be
there for legal reasons.) Then, you'll be given your dog's leash and
clip it to their collar.

When you take your dog out of their kennel and see or hear or feel their
tail wagging, you'll feel the entirely natural desire to fuss over them.
While you're distracted, a bag of supplies that couldn't possibly fit
everything it contains will be prepared for you, and a door will appear
nearby that *definitely* wasn't there before. This will take you
straight home (or to your car, if you can drive and by some miracle
found parking) so you and your new, formerly-human dog can get right
into your new life together.

You will have adopted a formerly human dog, as promised, and now you'll
have the assistant, companion, and pet you needed.

If you're here to get adopted, the process is much simpler. Once you
arrive at the appointed time and reaffirm that you want this, you'll be
taken to an empty kennel, which will have been outfitted with a mirror.

The person running this shelter (some part of you may already be
thinking of him as your handler for now) will step in with you, closing
the door behind them.

You'll look in the mirror and see a dog there.

Then your senses will catch up to you, telling you your body has changed
to match the reflection.

Your handler will put a collar around your neck and step away, leaving
you alone with nothing but water, bedding, and a mirror. You'll explore
the space and your new body thoroughly, but eventually settle down, as
there's nothing else you could do in this room.

Getting thrust into simply existing as a dog like this won't be pleasant
at first. You'll need to get used to the dull moments where there's
nothing to do, though you'll soon find them preferable to all the
moments you had too much to do. If you wouldn't have come to prefer this
life, you wouldn't be here, after all.

Some time after you arrive (hours, probably?), you'll start hearing a
commotion coming from your nearest neighbor. Your curiosity about that
will be resolved soon as you get your first meal as a dog. Whether
you'll still feel some embarrassment at eating kibble from a bowl on the
floor or whether you'll dive in to stave off a sense of hunger will be
up to you.

You'll get your first walk, too, but you'll only be led out back for
your first few days while the staff person — your handler — makes sure
you're settling in well before you're tempted by a city.

While you spend your first night in your new bed, that mirror you were
likely using to entertain yourself will be taken away. It will have
served its purpose. The next morning, along with breakfast, you'll get a
tag on your collar with your new name.

Over the next few days and weeks, your life will grow slightly less
sparse. You'll be introduced to your fellow formerly human dogs and
taught any basic aspects of being a dog you might not have been familiar
with in your past life. Once your handler gets a sense of what your
favorite toys are, he'll even put them in your kennel so you have
something to do during the long stretches when no one's around.

You'll also get trained on doggy manners, just in case. You almost
certainly have a decent idea of what a dog should or shouldn't do, but
your handler will want to be sure you know what isn't safe to eat and
that you know you'll get a treat for waiting patiently instead of trying
to open doors yourself like you used to be able to with no effort.

Soon enough, the time will blur together, punctuated by a rhythm of
food, walks, playtime, lessons, and, most importantly, visitors.
Visitors will be rare, but you'll be happy to see them. They're
different, and that's exciting, but, more importantly, they might be
your new owner ... or a new friend whose scent you'll get to know a
while from now.

Then, one day, long after you'd given up on trying to count them,
someone will visit you. They'll be back a few times, and then, like
you've heard before, they'll tell you about themselves. This time,
though, you'll feel it. You'll know in your heart that this person would
be the right owner for you. That the role they're asking you to take on
— that the kind of dog they need you to be — is the right fit for you.

It won't take your new owner long to realize that everything's coming
together. Then, after you don't object, they'll leash you up and take
you home with them.

You will have been adopted, as promised, and now you will have the life
as a dog you needed.
