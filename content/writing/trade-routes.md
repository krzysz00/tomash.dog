“I’m a bit old to go running around killing universe-destroying
abominations — that was the job, right?” Between several scars, the
touches of gray working their way into his mane, and that he was missing
part of his right ear, Aulhar Tauran looked like he had a point. “Heck,
I don’t think I could’ve won the challenge if I'd made it to the front
hall instead of…” He waved a hand at the corridor outside the Marquis’s
office.

*That is what most PPC agents do,* replied the telepathic daisy in a
bowler hat on the other side of the desk. *Though there are other
positions.*

“Do you need merchants?” Aulhar asked. This was a long shot, but maybe
this strange flower-person didn’t know that going out and trading just
“wasn’t appropriate for a man” then…

*Do you have any experience?*

Aulhar’s ears drooped forward. “No, but I’ve always wanted to try,” he
replied, hoping to keep the disappointment out of his voice.

*Perfect.* The Flower stared at the paperwork on his desk for a moment,
then finalized everything with a loud *CLUNK*. If Aulhar noticed the
stamp coming out of hammerspace, he didn’t react to it. *Finance keeps
bothering me for people, maybe this’ll get them off my back for five
seconds.*

A frond slid the forms back at Aulhar. *Mr. Tauran, get those over to
the Acquisitions Division, Department of Finance. They’ll take things
from here.*

“Huh? Wha’?”

*Acquisitions, Finance, don’t lose the paperwork.* The Marquis didn’t
appear to do anything, but Aulhar nevertheless found himself outside the
closed door to his office. For lack of directions, he picked a direction
and started walking.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

In true newbie fashion, Aulhar set out to find the Department of
Finance. While that would’ve normally led to a long wander through a
maze of twisty gray corridors, all alike, interrupted by finding
everything except DoF (such as the HQ pool), Aulhar managed to distract
himself, purely by accident. He’d been checking the room numbers, and
when he noticed that RC 112358 came between RC 271 and RC 3*i*, he
switched to picking random turns as he tried to work out what sort of
numbering scheme would’ve put those three rooms next to each other.

A few minutes later, without any recollection of how he’d gotten there,
the hani stood in front of a front counter helpfully labeled
‘Acquisitions Division’. A middle-aged man sat behind the counter,
typing away at his console.

“Hello?” Aulhar asked.

The man didn’t turn to look at Aulhar. “You have your 1020?”

Aulhar flipped through the folder he’d been given. “They didn’t give me
one.”

“There’s a bunch of them over on the wall.” Aulhar walked over to the
wall that looked impossibly crowded with stacks of blank forms and began
to look through them. Form 1020, ‘Request for Special Order’ had a lot
of subtypes, from completely understandable (1020-A: Armor) to bizarre
(1020-C: Cursed items) and utterly bewildering (1020-P: Miracles, divine
intervention).

Aulhar checked the form holders for something relevant, lifting out a
copy of each form. Finding nothing, he checked them all again and then
went back to the counter. “I don’t need a 1020,” he said, setting his
folder down. “I’m supposed to be working here. Also, why’s there a form
to order *WMDs*?”

The man turned around and took a look at Aulhar. “Oh, hey, they finally
sent new people!” he said, smiling as he stood up. “I’m Steve,” he said,
holding out a hand.

Aulhar didn’t accept the handshake, not being familiar with them, but
did bow slightly. “Aulhar Tauran,” he replied.

“It’s good to have you, Aulhar,” Steve said.

“Aulhar,” the hani corrected, emphasizing the pronunciation of the ‘lh’.

“Aulhar.” Steve repeated the name slowly. It
still wasn't quite right, but that was inevitable given his different
vocal structure.[^1]

“Close enough.”

“Anyway,” Steve glanced at the contents of the file from Personnel
before tucking it under his keyboard, “let me guess, they sent you over
here without telling you anything about the job?”

Aulhar nodded. “The Flower said you’d explain things.”

Steve sighed. “Typical PPC, then. Oh, and, since you asked, 1020-WMD is
there so we can quickly send all the agents who ‘urgently need their own
personal Sun Crusher’ straight to the trash.” He looked up to make sure
the Sar-Plasm hadn’t started leaking again, then put a ‘Back Later’ sign
on the counter. “Follow me,” he said, opening the back entrance, “I’ll
try to explain on the way to your RC.”

“So,” Steve said, once they were in a hallway. “What’ve you worked out
so far?”

“Bad writing somehow threatens the universe because of … plotholes?, and
PPC agents go fix up the damage, usually by killing whatever a Mary Sue
is. And Acquisitions tries to get them the stuff they need.” He waited a
moment for a response, then added “No idea how, though.”

Steve nodded. “That’s a pretty good summary for only being in HQ for an
hour or so. Plotholes aren’t always bad, though — there’s always some of
them around, and they’re how we travel around. The glowy blue portals
you’ll be able to call up from your console or with your RA — Remote
Activator, I think, can’t remember what it stands for — are all just
stable plotholes.”

*Would that mean…?* Aulhar had the beginnings of a realization, and
needed more information to see if he was on the right track. “How long’s
it take to go through one of these portals? And how far can you get?” he
asked.

“You can get anywhere just by stepping through,” Steve said.

Aulhar stopped, considered that information, and smiled. *Everyone back
home would* kill *for that,* he realized, *and I’m pretty sure I know
how they make money here.*

Steve looked back at the newbie once he noticed the lack of footsteps.
“I’m pretty sure that’s the ‘I just had a brilliant idea’ look, even
though I don’t know everything about lion-people’s facial expressions.”

“I think I just worked out a lot of how the PPC makes money!” Aulhar
said, gesturing excitedly. When Steve didn’t interrupt him, he
continued. “Let’s say there’s bad news on Anuun, maybe a copper mine
collapsed or something. That’ll drive prices up, but it takes time for
the news to get out to somewhere far out like Meetpoint. But, since we
can step over there much faster than the news, we can buy up a lot of
copper on the cheap and then profit once the prices go up. Or we load up
the stuff and start heading back normally with an amazing deal.”

Steve nodded and his eyebrows went up briefly. “Huh, Forbidden Plan
number 4. That’s not what most people think of first.”

“Forbidden Plan number 4?” Aulhar asked, quite confused. He hadn’t even
considered something like that as a possible reaction.

“Yeah. A lot of the ideas people have for making money with PPC tech
make nasty plotholes, either really big ones or a whole lot of tiny
ones. Too much of that and it’ll break the universe we’re trying to
trade in.”

“Which is bad,” Aulhar confirmed.

“Which is bad *and* might make Legal remember we exist.” Steve added.
“So, Forbidden Plan #4: ‘No front-running’.”

Aulhar nodded. “So the job’s finding ways to make money without making
plotholes?”

“Yeah. Want to have a guess at the rest of the Forbidden Plans?” Steve
said. “It’ll cut down on the infodumping.”

Aulhar was silent for a moment. “Oh! Portal right into a bank vault,
take everything, and disappear?”

“Number 3: ‘No implausible crimes or accidents’, yep.” Steve said. “Good
catch.”

Aulhar didn’t have any other ideas that didn’t seem to fall under the
other two categories. He started walking again, which set Steve moving.
The pair worked their way deeper into the Finance RCs, and Aulhar
eventually admitted, “I can’t think of anything else. Maybe because I’ve
never actually been out trading before.”

“You’ll get the hang of it,” Steve said. “We all started somewhere. Now,
here’s a hint: portals don’t just go through space.”

Before Aulhar had finished processing the hint, a dragon stormed down
the cross corridor holding an open book out as if it was trying to bite
them. “No, no, no!” they shouted, “There’s *no way* bread and oil prices
change relative to each other that much in *two* *\[bleeeep\]ing
months*!” Aulhar wondered how they’d made a beeping noise with their
mouth.

“Wait, could you buy something that used to be expensive but then got
cheap and take it back in time and sell it there? And then you could
take the money back to the future, buy more of the thing, bring it to
the past and … but then eventually it’d stop being expensive in the past
and …” Aulhar looked at Steve. “That’s what you meant by breaking the
universe, right?”

“Yeah, the time travel inflation trick is … they didn’t even try it back
in the old days when the rules were a lot looser. And so Forbidden Plan
#2: ‘No time travel for profit.’”

Aulhar went back to trying to work out what Forbidden Plan #1 would be,
trailing quietly behind the person who somehow seemed able to navigate
around here. Soon, Steve stopped in front of a door that looked just
like the rest of them. “RC 778,” he announced. “This one’s yours now.”

RC 778 looked like it’d just been furnished, though the dust and cobwebs
made it clear its contents had been sitting there for a while. The
entrance opened onto an office/living room. One wall was dominated by a
desk, most of which was taken up by the console, while the other side of
the room was mainly empty aside from the large dark green couch and the
two chairs that had been wheeled in haphazardly. Behind the office,
Aulhar could see a bedroom with two beds and a pair of nightstands and a
bathroom. None of the sheets matched each other, as if whoever had been
putting in the furniture had been grabbing them out of a box without
much care.

*\[bip\]* . As Aulhar stepped into his new home, the console went off.

“That was fast,” Steve remarked.

*\[bip\] \[bip\]*.

“That’s just the mail, you can get to it later,” he added as Aulhar took
one of the chairs. Steve took the other. Aulhar began trying to work out
what the maze of knobs, levers, and switches on the underside of this
chair did, while Steve adjusted his seat into a comfortable position
after bouncing up and down a few times.

“Will someone else be living here?” Aulhar asked. “It looks like there
should be.”

“Probably not. Acquisition missions don’t usually need a partner, but
we’ve ended up with two-person RCs anyway.”

“Typical PPC?” Aulhar asked.

Steve chuckled. “You sure you’re new?”

“I’ve heard the family complaining about bureaucrats enough times,”
Aulhar said. He leaned back in his chair, having found which of the mess
of levers let him do that. “So, what *will* I be doing?”

“We use plotholes that’re already around to make money or get useful
things. One big one’s …” Steve put up a hand. “Hold on, I just realized
I’ve got no idea where you’re from—you heard of video games?”

“The first person I met said something about the Chanur Saga and the
Alliance-Union 'verse—I’ve never heard of an Alliance or a Union, and
I’ve got no idea what a large trading family I’ve never dealt with has
to do anything.”

Steve fiddled with something electronic he pulled out of a pocket.
“Looks like we’ve got some—” *\[bip\]* “—books to read. I sent us both
the list.”

“But yes, I’ve played some video games,” Aulhar said. He looked over at
the console. “That thing ever stop beeping?”

*\[bip.\]* The console sounded annoyed.

“Right, good. Ever noticed how, in a lot of games, the shops somehow
reset? You can take all of their stuff and eventually more shows up?”

“Huh, yeah. You saying all that goes here?”

“Not quite. Universes are good at filling in plausible details, but
sometimes the gold or armor or whatever just … falls down a plothole.
And if we *just happened* to replace a shopkeeper for a bit and then
*just happened* to disappear with everything … that’s normal enough.”

“Replace?” Aulhar asked, ears back. “I thought we didn’t kill people in
Finance.”

“Nah, we’ve got disguise generators and can usually talk people into
taking a vacation.”

Aulhar let out a sigh of relief. “Makes sense. How do I start?”

Steve stood up. “I’ll go ahead and show you how to work the buttons,
then point you at a good first run.”

Aulhar stood up and took a few steps to stand next to Steve in front of
the console.

“Oh, and since we didn’t get to them, Forbidden Plan #1 is ‘No bringing
anything where it doesn’t belong’ and #5 is ‘No supernatural deals’.”

Aulhar nodded. “I can’t believe I didn’t think of moving between
universes!! Though where’d number 5 come from?”

“We don’t want anyone getting cursed for their greed,” Steve said.

“… right, stories are real, that can actually happen.” Aulhar looked
around, taking in the alien he’d been talking to for a while now, the
computer unlike he’d ever worked with, and the abundance of gray.
“*Gods*, today’s been weird.”

“Oh, it’s just getting started.”

------------------------------------------------------------------------

One training montage later, Aulhar found himself behind the counter of a
shop in a snowy mountain city. He didn’t quite look like himself, but
the changes weren’t particularly jarring. Before he had time to get
settled in properly, the door to his store opened, revealing someone
nearly overflowing with what could only be described as dungeon loot.
Finance’s newest agent smiled as he felt the logic of the universe
pulling on him.

“Khajiit has wares, if you have coin.”

[^1]: For those playing along at home, “Awllar”, pronounced as if it were a Welsh word, is a pretty close approximation.
